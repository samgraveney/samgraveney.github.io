Ruby's Nose

RUBY'S NOSE

I can tell by the rising sun it's time to patrol. I take my companion out early now, he isn't sleeping well. He harnesses himself to me, leashing us together, necessary because his senses are inferior to mine. I don't mind guiding him. He opens the door to the world and steps out hurriedly, but I look over my shoulder for her, because I can smell her, but she's not coming on the walk. I had forgotten. Then I trot ahead of him. I must go first.

It's early, it's hot, evaporating dewdrops carry odours to my nose. I bathe in scent. Sheets drying behind rows of houses, clean and soft and I want to push my face against them for the caress. I smell crows, which smell of all meats in one, and of tree sap on their claws. I smell the sluggish river ahead, and I smell food in lidded bins, a smell which throbs whenever, up and down our long street, a human throws out something worth investigating. But my companion and I are on patrol. We have purpose, intention, direction. I must go first. I hunt cats under cars.

I respect my companion, by the way. He doesn't see as clearly as me, or hear as well, or smell as keenly (each weakness daily demonstrated by a missed chicken bone at his feet or an overlooked squirrel by his head). But he can see over walls, and can manipulate objects. I take the important duties, mainly security and scouting, but he pays his way. He scratches me where I can't reach. He gives me food as tribute. We sleep in shifts. He first brought her into our house, a priceless gift (though I think I charmed her too). I don't know which of us is older, but generally wherever we go, I take responsibility, I go first. I love him very dearly.

We pass through the gate to the grass expanse at the end of our road. I am careful to mark our territory at this chokepoint, squatting, squirting, scanning the environment. My companion rarely marks anywhere except one spot inside, and the scent is immediately annihilated by running water. You see what I mean about my duties outweighing his? I mark trees, metal poles, bins, even sticks other dogs have discarded but which serve as loci of communication. I let all know we are here, fit and ready, and that all pass through only with our blessing.

She didn't smell healthy, towards the end. She smelt bad. Once, I found a piece of bread under my bed, sharp and stiff with electric-blue mould. She smelt like that, like that but left to burn in her stomach. I was the first to detect it. It took them longer to notice. She came on fewer walks after that.

Dogs! Both familiar, one black, one brindled, both big and slow and currently judged friendly, guiding a round human who smells of blackberries and bacon and who often congratulates me for this or that feat with gentle hands behind my ears.

My companion greets theirs, using the limited but beautiful human music-language. I greet the two dogs much more efficiently, establishing good health by nose and gland, tails lifted obligingly, bellies to the sun and legs splayed, better to share the news of the day. My companion's face grows hot and salty and his eyes are wet like an old dog's. The big round man was at our house yesterday. It was a disorienting day. Everyone wore dark clothes and shoes were black and oily, and she lay scentless in a box and then she was gone.

We dogs run. In the summer, when the ground is baked hard, I have purchase. I can go and go, my front legs on strong shoulders clawing the metres under me, passing them back to my steel-sinewed haunches which drum the dirt to dust. I am launched onwards, ears flattened, hearing gone, scents incomprehensible as my speed forces air down my nose, eyes watering, I move by instinct and by glimpse. A dog is usually assailed by the sense of the outside, but not when I run. My two playmates cannot keep up, of course they can't. I know he is watching. I know he delights in me running, and that makes me happy.

I am too hot. I trot to the river and wallow and pant, limbs folded, water leaching heat, moist river air soothing lungs. When my companion gets hot he goes salty, but not like earlier, from his eyes only. He struggles to regulate his emotions like I do.

He's been angry with me, even before she stopped and went away. Her fragrance is still in the house, but the artificial smells are the ones which endure most and evoke least, like the scented water she sprayed from a little bottle. It is harder and harder to find the smell of her that is her, the smell of the backs of her knees and the inside of her mouth and her hair. Last night I climbed onto the big and complicated bed she had slept in, stopped in, and I smelt her, stronger even than the blue-mould smell, I smelt her and she was there. I rubbed my cheeks and gums on her pillow to mingle our scents, and he found me and pulled me from the bed by my collar and hurt me.

I rise dripping from the water. My companion has come to me, doubtless feeling blind without me to point the way. He has the ball he practices his throwing with. He throws, I hunt. We are a pack, creatures working and living together, working all the harder now our three is two. He extends a hand to take the ball back, but I parade with it a little, aware of other dogs and humans admiring. But he should have his fun too: I return it, he throws it.

A big red dog with a stripe of fur down its spine approaches the ball where it lies, rocking a little, alive and desirable and my blood pumps a little faster as this new dog threatens my prey. I curl my lips, corrugating the flesh around my nose, baring my teeth. This dog is bigger, but it is my ball, our ball, and I widen my stance and hunch my shoulders. My tail is up and out, and I am certain I look ferocious, and I make a liquid growl deep in my chest, and I am certain I sound ferocious. I will not let this dog take our ball. I prepare to pounce.

Then the dog's companion is there, clinging to it, restraining it lest I knock it down. Supplicant, the companion gives me the ball. Victor, I march, back straight, tail erect, high-stepping and noble, shaking the ball like the rats I could surely catch. Then I cannot contain my euphoria. I drop the ball, and break into a sprint. I am seized with a molten sense of ecstasy that comes from my body in the sun and my speed and power. It is divine, and I feel it, usually, once per patrol. He and she seemed to experience something similar, if less frequently; usually at night and in their bed, their hearts racing, their scents hot and surprising and not the stale mouth-smells of sleep. They would defend their joy quite fiercely, pushing me away when I jumped onto the bed to be involved. 

I am running for the joy of it, and again my senses collapse into me, me, me, and my heart runs in my chest, thundering so loud, and it recalls to me how hers had got fainter and fainter and had stopped, in the night, in her strange room I was barred from, her sinister room of angled metal and clear bags of liquid. I heard her heart stop. Panicking, I scratched him awake, drew blood, so he could operate the doors, so we could help her. But she was still and she was quiet, and though she smelt of her, she did not sound like her. No heartbeat, no tide of breath, no susurration of hair on hair. And I was so intensely focused on her, so desperate to hear that beloved heart resume, it was like I had gone deaf and all noise was gone from the world. He shook her, and she gurgled a little, wheezed a little, but these were the false noises of the false animals with squeakers, not the sounds of life. He sat on her bed and put his face in his hands and I think even he knew she had stopped.

I have stopped running, because my heart is breaking, and I can smell her very clearly for a moment. It is my collar. Some mote of her in my collar has risen to my nose, and she's there, and I am ashamed I lay in the river, adulterating her memory with duck-scent and grit. 

He finds me despairing, contorting, because I can't force my nose any closer to my collar. I am pleased to be with him, and it is almost like he is looking after me when he takes up the leash and strokes my ears. For a few paces, he must go first.

We walk for hours. It's hot, and the salt from his eyes mixes with the salt from his scalp. My paws ache and crack and grass seeds chafe between my toes. We have no water and my mouth tastes foul. Neither of us want to go home. We live in a haunted house now. I smell through time and space and when a door opens or a blanket is shaken out she is there again, and gone again. I feel her presence as a phantom pain. I can't bear her scent, nor bear to lose it.

I'm not naive about stopping. I came into the world smelling of it (I shared the womb with a stillborn brother). But most stopping has a little life to it: the hedgehog's quills twitch with maggots, the kingfisher's feathers shift under the tread of ants. Not so for her. She lay so completely and sterilely still, then, sealed in a scentless bag, was taken by men who smelt of nothing and whose saltless faces made them nearly invisible in a salt sea of spectators. How can so absolute a disappearance leave behind so many splinters?

He opens the door to our home. Her ghost is there, in the dark and the cool. He hesitates. He still has his leash in his hand. I will guide him in because he depends on me. I will go first.
