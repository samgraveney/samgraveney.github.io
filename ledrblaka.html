LEDRBLAKA

The big man stood in the twilit surf, facing the family and panting. They had not seen him approach; they had been whelking. He had come from the sea, but there was no boat. In one hand he held a hatchet, in the other, a dagger. He did not move.

Father drew himself to his full height, and pulled his knife from his belt, a mere tool, no weapon. Son looked about, scrabbling in the cold, wet sand, and found a smooth pebble, the right size for his hand. He stood a little behind Father. Mother clutched toddling Daughter.

Father levelled the knife, twisting his body to present a narrower target, dredging up what training he remembered from the war. The man with the axe and the dagger still had not moved, his breathing slowing, his face neutral.

Father asked what the man wanted, said they had nothing worth taking. No answer came.

The man took a step forward, and another, out of the surf. Big, heavy steps, knife and axe swinging limply at his sides. Spray hit Father in the face, and he retreated, knocking into Son. They both fell. Mother darted forward and pulled Father to his feet with her thin arms and hysterical strength. She was trembling to the point of vibrating. Daughter was crouched on the sand, 'being little,' as Mother had taught her when violence came from the sea.

The man with axe and dagger stumbled forwards again. For all his violent motion his expression had not changed from its dead neutrality. Though he faced them, he barely seemed aware of them.

Son lurched to his feet, had backed away, had wound his arm back almost behind his head, and had loosed the pebble. It struck the man beneath his vacant right eye. As it struck, the man opened his mouth, and the bone-noise of stone on skull came out from the void, the closest he had come to speech. Blood welled and ran out of him too fast as it diluted in the seawater on his face and raced down his neck and under his ragged shirt.

For a moment, it looked as if the blood was retreating, back up, back into the cut, as a shadow rose from his throat to his chin. Not shadow, but short dun hair. The man's face furred like mould on meat. The gash, horizontal, under his eye, stretched and split as the man's face elongated. His nose shrank, flattened and blackened. The eyes separated and darted to either side of the long face. His ears climbed on top of his head and stretched for Heaven.

Though he kept a man's body, the head was a man-sized rabbit's. The fur darkened under the side-facing eye as the gash kept bleeding. The mouth flapped open and big rabbit teeth gnashed. The ears pivoted about wildly. Mother groaned. Father cursed, then the curse turned into a mutter of horror as the rabbit-man stepped forward. Father extended his knife, meekly warding, not striking, but it continued, and the knife sank into its belly. It stopped and slowly turned its head to so one big, watery, blank eye was fixed on Father.

Again, its face changed. Again, its head moved to keep Father fixed in sight as the eyes moved across the rabbit skull like bubbles under ice. The fur receded, leaving black mottled skin, and the rabbit's snout became half-human, half-pig. The big ears turned forwards, became bald and fleshy and ribbed like a longship's hull. The mouth widened into a grin, and when it opened a hundred needle teeth flashed. From the mouth came a terrible sound that only Son and Daughter could hear, a screeching they recognised, but had never heard so loud. The man had the head of a bat.

Daughter had liked bats, she had liked that they were allowed to stay up later than her, and loved the moonlight. This bat seemed to love nothing, was aware of nothing. She screamed and screamed and screamed.

Mother shouted at Father. His hand was still on the knife handle which was still in the creature's stomach. He was gazing at the bat with dread. But he heard her shout, and he heard Daughter\'s shriek, and he acted. He pulled the knife back, then slid it slowly and firmly under the creature's chin.

The knife was too short and too blunt to pierce the skull, but the damage was done. The short axe and the long dagger dropped from the creature's hands, never used, never even brandished. Blood drizzled from its throat down Father's forearm. And the face changed one last time. The snout became longer than ever, with a big, wide leathery tip. The eyes grew, and long eyelashes surrounded them. The fur returned, coarser than the rabbit's. A strip of longer hair sprouted between tall ears. It had become a donkey. Its soft lips slobbered over the father's arm, seeking its own blood. It died silently and without complaint.

As the creature collapsed, it disappeared, leaving only the crumple of its clothes, which bobbed in the surf. Father looked at the blood on his blade, and the blood which had run down his arm and, at the crook of his elbow, dropped onto his feet. He squatted to wash his hands in the sea, suddenly clenching his eyes shut and gasping and trembling. Son moved forwards and picked up the dead creature's shirt, holding it by the shoulders to investigate.

A fleshy lump fell out from the shirt and splatted into the wet sand between dagger and axe. The boy crouched, and lifted it up, holding it by the tips of its wings. Dead, the evening breeze made it jerk. Rabbit teeth forced their way from the small bat mouth. Black bat legs terminated in hooves. And the frames which held its wings taut sprouted ten white human fingernails at their tips.
