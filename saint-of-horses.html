Saint of Horses

SAINT OF HORSES

Paul the Tall was the finest archer in the king\'s army. On the march, he entertained soldiers by shooting birds on the wing with his green-fletched arrows. He dyed the fletching himself, so he could claim his kills. Sometimes he shot doves.

One day, Paul the Tall led his six-strong band into a village with a sandstone church. The sun was at their backs, muddied by smoke from a corpse-pyre they had built. Paul, of course, was the first to see the French scout watering his horse in the churchyard. He lamed the horse with a shot through the front left knee, forcing it to bow, forcing its hind legs to kick out uselessly and divot the dirt. A second arrow shimmered into the scout's belly, a slow-killing shot.

Paul the Tall ambled towards his victims over untended scrub, past overgrown graves (all the gardeners in Brittany were dead). The horse was screaming, the scout was praying.

'Saint Eloi!' the scout gasped. 'Saint Eloi!', groping towards a sandstone saint holding crosier and horseshoe staring down at him with cold eyes from the church's sandstone lintel.

Paul the Tall did not speak the scout's language, but mean Vester did, and mean Vester squatted by the man and interrogated him with French and a knife.

\'There\'s some fool duke out here. A farm, to the north, Paul.\'

Paul the Tall left the bleeding scout with his ruined and wretched horse in the shadow of the corpse-smoke and the empty church, and strode north, followed by mean Vester and his band.

The duke was easy to find. He, too, had a horse, and a loud one. It whickered and it huffed and it whinnied and its tail cracked like a whip. But the duke, a boy really, was not alone. Fifteen men, in hauberks and helmets, with big shields and spears.

'Trepan his skull, we\'ll run for it,\' grinned mean Vester. \'He\'s the only one with a mount anyway.\'

Paul the Tall nodded slowly. \'Ready yourselves. It\'ll be a running fight back to camp, but I bet there\'s a prize for a dead duke.\'

They squatted in a copse, a little way from the boy-duke and the soldiers. Paul plunged two green-fletched arrows into the dirt.

\'First the beast, then the boy. You dogs set?\'

Vester and the band were. Fat yew wood and oiled gut, steel bodkin heads and ash shafts, and the feathers of hawk and goose and sap glue, the armoury of the yeoman archer, assembled for the kill. They waited for Paul the Tall to begin. He plucked an arrow from the ground as if he were picking a flower, twirling it in rough fingers, nocked and drew. Seventy yards, an easy shot. His vast, lopsided archer shoulders held the tension. He aimed at the horse.

Saint-Eloi, gasped the French breeze.

Paul loosed. The arrow went wide, missing the duke's big black mare. He did not see where it went, for he was groping for his second arrow. Nock, ready, loose. A perfect shot for where the duke had been one hearbeat earlier. But, alerted, the French were moving.

'Loose! Kill them!' shouted mean Vester, as Paul, baffled by his failure, hesitated. But the French had their shields up and the duke and mare were gone.

It was a shambling, stumbling, wheezing, panicked flight, a race of twisted ankles and splitting sides and sweat. Paul the Tall fell behind, long legs becoming heavy, and he watched mean Vester and his band tear away, pursued by armoured men. He fled back to the churchyard, back to the scout and the moaning horse.

'Saint Eloi,' the scout whimpered. He was still alive. 'Eau, eau, Saint-Eloi.'

Paul had heard that word before, from prisoners. He knew what it meant, and ignored it. The scout was propped against his horse, hands on his belly, pale as teeth, wincing as the crippled beast\'s breathing raised him up and dropped him down and squeezed a little more blood out of him. The stone saint ignored the scout but his sandstone eyes in his sacred sandstone skull transfixed the archer, and Paul forced the door to the church to escape it. He could hear hooves on the wind.

The church was barren, its wealth taken beyond the reach of Englishmen. Only one faded tapestry remained. Paul smashed a pew, took a stave, barred the church doors, muffling the horse's groans and Saint-Eloi hissed through bloodless lips outside. He could feel hoofbeats, or perhaps it was his heart pounding.

The tapestry showed a wool-woven man holding a bow. Somehow, in flight, Paul had kept his own, and two arrows. By the woven man was a woven horse with three legs and white agonised eyes. Paul could hear hoofbeats now, but the Saint-Eloi Saint-Eloi from outside was too loud, too fast, to be coming from the gut-shot scout. The woven fourth horse's leg, the front left, was on the woven grass, bloody. Saint-Eloi Saint-Eloi Saint-Eloi the hooves drummed out at a gallop. Paul stared at the fourth leg, mouth dry. It sprouted a woven arrow and the green of the fletching alone in the faded tapestry was emerald-bright. The churchyard amplified the hoofbeats and Saint-Eloi Saint-Eloi Saint-Eloi Saint-Eloi reverberated around his skull louder than an army's raised voice, louder than the sea, louder than anything, loud as hell. Woven Virgin, woven child, and a woven saint with woven horseshoe and crosier all pointed in damnation at the woven man, but Paul the Tall felt their woven eyes on him.

When the black mare kicked down the church doors all the duke could find was a green-fletched arrow stabbed through a tapestry, transfixing a horse, and a smashed window through which the Englishman must have fled.

The scout's vision was dim, and he looked up at sandstone Saint Eloi who had watched over the village for countless generations.

Please, saint of horses, he prayed, water for me, mercy for Brix, he was a good horse.

A green-fletched arrow came from the heavens. Brix stopped moaning.

Merci.
