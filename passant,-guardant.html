Passant, Guardant

The Bosphorus, 1204

I will dress in the dark, a custom of many years. No servants in my cabin. No one needs to see my frailty. I raise two bony legs from my bed and stand up. No slippers and no carpets, either. I want to feel the Hungarian oak under my feet.Crossing the room, I am a little unsteady. I tell myself it is the motion of the Bosphorus, not old age, and drop my scarlet robe over my nightshirt. The gold lions of St. Mark, passant, guardant, in stitching, strut down my thin shoulders. They are heavy, but I bear them. I run my trembling, thrilled, grasping fingertips over the buckles my mail coat, but reject it. I am Venetian, and my ship is my fortress. I do not need armour. But I do take my sword. I am told it shines magnificently.

I climb to the deck, following the moist air. At first all feels fresh and glorious, but I smell death on the wind and blood in the water. I hear a whole city groaning and bells tolling like a racing heartbeat. I glance about, showing all on board my face, and I listen. My whole life, I have been able to follow many conversations simultaneously, a skill ever more useful in old age. My Italians, my crew, discussing practical matters: how to help soldiers cross the waterway from the citadel at Galata, how to carry long ladders in little boats. My surly Greek prisoners, interrogated, explaining the cunning currents dancing out of the Black Sea. And the bad French of my tame, one-handed Englishman, grizzling to his Norman colleague. I approach, gliding, bare toes nudging rope and splinter aside. I swallow, because I am salivating. I drench myself in hauteur to dampen my excitement.

'What do I see?' I ask, in perfect French, improved by the accent of Veneto. I point my face at the Englishman.

'The fleet is deployed.'

My fleet.

'The ships are taking arrows from the walls, but no Greek fire.'

No flames yet.

'The weather has cleared. The men can cross.'

Cross and take the city. For me.

'Where is Galata?' I ask. My mouth is watering again.

'To your right.'

'He knows where Galata is,' mutters the Norman. 'He knows everything.'

An insult, and the truth.

'At the very least, I know you Franks should have crossed from Galata as soon as the storm ended.' The shuffling of metal links, as if mice were lowering an anchor chain. 'And if you will exchange private glances, wait until you are not wearing mail.'

'No crusaders have landed. Yet,' comes the Norman's defensive reply. 'Our kings wait for the right moment to attack. Or, better, they wait for the emperor to come to his senses and surrender.'

I wave a hand, dismissive, but do not see their reaction. 'We will take the City. Today. By force.' We are so close. A gust of warm wind brings the smell of smoke, and the warning of worsening weather. 'No room for cowardice on my crusade.'

///

His crusade? Are we that cadaver's servants? For all my anger, I manage to catch the Norman's dour eye without moving my neck. We grimace and swallow yet another insult. Ambassadors, we swallow a lot. I mute a sigh, then a shout, as an arrow strikes the deck. The Doge does not see it but I follow its trajectory, up. High up. The City's striped defences are twice as tall as the masts of our - his - ships. The Greek's Varangians are twice as tall as our champions (it was one of them who took my hand at the Romanus Gate). The walls throb with defenders. The Norman said the City must be empty, every man on the battlements, but he's wrong. Constantinople is the City. Millions dwell within. Seat of emperors, bastion against the heathen Turk and pagan Bulgar. I am sickened, not by sea-swell or death-stench, but by our blasphemy. Like Gog and Magog, we are locked out of Paradise, and will kill Christians to get in. All for one man's blind ambition.

The Doge's ship Bucintoro leads a fleet of a hundred. One hundred floating castles, bristling with iron, like great urchins storm-snatched from the sea bed. But below the walls, we are dwarfed. Bolts and arrows drum on the deck, and wound reluctant soldiers. It is like they are missiles from Heaven. We deserve them. We left the West for God, not Constantinople. First, the Doge offered aid, which mutated into debt, and then twisted into obligation. The Hungarians, killed for their oak, know what we were obliged to do.

I watch him. He drags his feet on the deck so as not to fall and holds in his claws a child's sword. His eyes are still, but his mouth is jabbering, as ever, and his flunkeys approach. They conspire and then, to my horror, he is taken to the flagship's tender, followed by a squad of squat marines. He gives an exhortation, but my Italian is bad and the wind is up.

'What the fuck is he doing?' asks the Norman.

The tender drops from sight. Around us, the Doge's urchins squirt out overloaded little rowboats, packed with more men. Madness. Cross from Galata, where the water is still! The Bosphorus will take you all! I want to scream, but the Venetians know their business. I want try to pray, but I do not know whether I want the Doge to live or die. He sits neatly, a small figure, receding, but the sword is raised and glints like tin in the sea haze. The Greeks have seen this new approach, and more arrows fizz the water.

'He's forgotten his siege ladders.' The Norman's arms are crossed, but a vein in his temple throbs. He is not the only one stung by the Doge's valour. Action at Galata, still to the Doge's right. The crusaders, from Nottingham and Stafford and the Auvergne and Bohemia, witness an old man approaching the Greek's walls. Ladders sprout. Boats surge. The crossing begins. But they will not be the first. The first to land on the beach at the base of the walls is Doge Enrico Dandolo. He swings thin legs over the gunwale, plants bare feet on the golden sand. Completely blind, the old man cannot see the prize he is about to claim.
