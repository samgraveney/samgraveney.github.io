ADMIRAL FOGGO IN THE DESERT

Admiral Foggo woke up in a pile of sand. He had a terrific headache and
felt nauseous. He was lost in a desert, and so concluded he had a
condition called sandsickness which, though fictional, affected him
badly.

He stood up carefully and slithered down the dune a little way. He
sucked his teeth and rotated on the spot. Nothing but yellow sand and
blue sky and the sun, high up.

'It's damn hot,' he said to himself. He wiped his sweating brow on his
navy woollen Navy jacket and looked down at his navy Navy trousers and
his black Navy shoes. He was bareheaded. The prince's men must have
taken his white Navy hat. The sandsickness was intolerable.

'I believe it is because I am so used to the motion of the sea, I must
have inverted sea legs.' Admiral Foggo, a scientific man, hypothesised,
and then tested. 'I will have to walk to safety. My sandsickness is
psychosomatic, a consequence of my life on the sea. Therefore, I must
fool my own brain.' He pictured the lubbers he had known, who had gone
green even at the sight of his first command, the sleek and lethal white
cutter named *Happy Pig.* He imitated their wide-splayed posture, hands
held out for balance, and took a couple of rolling steps. He felt the
sandsickness abate a little, and looked about again. On ships, sometimes
things appear over the horizon without much effort on one's own part.
But he had not moved. He did not know where to move to.

'\"*If one does not know to which port one is sailing, no wind is
favorable*". Seneca.' This was addressed to himself, not Seneca, who had
been dead for 1,965 years. But he was fond of naval aphorisms. Foggo
could tell north and south and up and down by the stars. He could tell
Pacific from Atlantic only by the merest glance at chart and compass.
But cast into the desert by the wrathful prince, he was all at sea.

'The prince is an Arab. Therefore it is likely I am in Arabia, as that
is where I was with him recently. I am familiar with the Red Sea and the
Persian Gulf. They tend to be south, west and east of most of Arabia. I
must merely not walk north, and I shall reach a coast. No doubt I can
commandeer a fisherboat and be on my way. So, let me find north, and
walk from it. This repulsive desert shall be my open road, and carry me
to the sea. By my lack of shadow, it is either night or midday. By the
presence of the sun, midday. Its verticality also suggests I am near the
equator, so I am indeed likely in Arabia.' It all fell into place. 'I
will wait until it dips, for that shall be west.' He paused. 'I am sure
the sun sets in the west even in Arabia. I would have heard otherwise.'

He sat back down and fell asleep, and woke in the blackest night. There
was no hint of the sun at any point on the horizon.

'Ah. An error on my part.' He glanced about, making sure no one had seen
him, but no one had, because he was lost in the desert. 'The North Star
will do.' He located it easily, squashed between his friends the Little
and Large Dippers. He turned his back to it and began to walk, and then
tripped over his rucksack.

'Aha. Jetsam.' He discovered rum, half-drained, likely by villains, and
a white glove. He shouldered his burden, turned his back to the North
Star, and weighed anchor.

He made it fifty feet before he was bitten by a snake. He sighed. He had
taken a bullet at the Battle of Santiago de Cuba, from one of his own
enlisted. A snake bite was much less serious. He picked up the snake by
its head and looked at it with disappointment. 'You'll regret that,
serpent. My blood is more salt than iron after a lifetime on the seas.
You're in for a thirsty night.' He dropped it, and it slithered away,
south.

'The snake shall mark my road. It will carry a bit of me ahead of me.'
Now snakesick and still a little sandsick, he advanced. After many hours
of following the snake road, the sun rose on his left. Admiral Foggo
called out to the snake, 'you see? I can navigate just as well here as
on the seas.'

The snake ignored him. It wound sideways, which Foggo ascribed to it
being salt-drunk. 'I bet you've never even seen the sea.'

He remembered saying something similar to the prince. That damn prince!
All Foggo had done was praise the sea and criticise the desert. And for
that, he had been exiled to the sands. He frowned and reached for the
white glove.

'It's the prince's glove!' The snake was still not interested. 'I must
have snatched it in exchange for my white Navy hat.' He trudged on, in
companionable silence with the snake, for many hours.

'It's damn hot. Must be midday.' He paused to sip a little rum. When he
looked up, the snake had gone, burrowing for cooler sand. 'Ah, you
coward.'

He pressed on. The snakesickness went, and he saw a cactus that was
shaped like a fat man holding a cigar in a wide hand. A small brown
bundle was at the fat man's feet. Foggo approached it.

'What are you? Some sort of desert seal?' The baby camel squinted at him
warily. 'Aha, I know you're a camel. Are you sandsick too?'. There was
no response. 'Have some rum.'

He fed the young camel from the bottle of rum, and it mewed. He picked
it up and gave it a close look. 'I suppose you want to come with me.'
Admiral Foggo was taking command of the enlisted. Naval life was not
solitary, and he was unused to being alone. The camel's long neck draped
over his shoulder. It was heavy. But he had someone to talk to on the
desert road.

'I don't know why the prince objected. This desert is dreadful.'

The camel's silence was a rebuke.

'I could have been more polite, I know.'

It coughed.

'He did invite me to his home. But why invite a naval man if you don't
want to hear about the sea?'

It shivered.

'You can't be cold, sailor. It is an oven out here. Not like the open
ocean. The sea breeze! This place is a land lacking the thinnest
stream.'

He advanced, waddle-like, relentless as the waves. Admiral Foggo would
not be kept from the water. He would march from the desert. The camel
squirmed, and he put it down and gave it more rum, medicinally. He had
some too, medicinally. They walked together, until Foggo paused, seeing
something sausage-shaped in the sand.

'Is *that* a desert seal?' The camel didn't know, so they walked towards
it. It was another camel, big and dead. Foggo knew it was the little
camel's mother. They looked alike.

'Condolences,' he said. When the sailor who had shot him had visited him
in the *Happy Pig'*s hospital, they had discussed naval strategy to
avoid recrimination. He tried the same thing with the camel.

'The prince invited me here to discuss whether naval theory could be
applied to desert warfare.'

The camel did not have a strong opinion on this, or was distracted, or
grieving, and Admiral Foggo knew when to be quiet. In the silence which
followed, as they walked through the desert, on the clear wind Foggo's
ears detected hoofbeats.

'Ahoy,' he muttered, slowly, eyes following where ears pointed. Stuffing
the camel down the front of his woolen jacket, he crept to the top of a
dune. A pack of horsemen, advancing. Their steeds were sweating, eyes
wide and wild, foaming at the mouth. Their riders were swathed in cloth,
faces obscured, but swords and lances and rifles adorned high saddles.

'A war party! No doubt come to finish me off!' Foggo dropped to his
belly, squashing the camel, which mewed.

The mew carried. It was piercing, like a ship's whistle. There was a
command in Arabic, and a horse reared, slowed, stopped, turned. A
turbaned head with green flashing eyes turned, hands pulling at the
reins. One was gloved in white. The other was bare.

'The prince! You damn fool, you've killed us both!' Foggo was accustomed
to collective punishment, as when a ship goes down the sea takes no
prisoners. He assumed the camel would perish too. Then he remembered
where he was. He plucked the creature from his jacket and placed it down
on its unsteady, foal-like legs.

'Run! Save yourself!'

The camel took two steps and tumbled.

'Goddamn this blasted sandsickness!' Foggo rose. The prince saw him, and
waved his lance. The horsemen circled, and began bearing down on him.
Foggo scooped up the camel and began to run, the sandsickness driving
him to his knees again and again. The sun bleached all strength from
him. The sand sucked at his feet. The dry air tore at his throat, and
the hoofbeats grew louder. A shadow fell over him, a rider on a horse.

'Admiral, are you feeling quite alright? You had us worried!'

Foggo staggered, fell, clutching his four-legged crewmate to his heart.

'Strike! Kill me but spare my crew!' He knelt up, eyes shut, awaiting
the fatal blow.

'Admiral, please! You're quite delirious.' He opened one eye. The prince
was standing before him, dismounted. His horse was breathing a little
heavily, but otherwise was demure. One of the prince's grooms caught up
with them and fed it an apple. 'You have had us terribly worried.'

'Eh?'

The prince gave a command in Arabic, and the groom gently took the young
camel, gave it water, and placed it in a saddlebag.

'Last night - I must say, I have never seen a man drink as much as you
did. You were quite lively.'

'We fought, did we not?'

The prince helped him to his feet.

'Only with words. The merits of the sea and the desert. You have a
poet's heart. My youngest son, I am afraid, you convinced to become a
sailor with your descriptions. He is already on the train to Alexandria.
You wrote him a letter of recommendation to the British *attache.'*

'And then what did I do?' asked Foggo, eyes narrowing. Perhaps he was
beginning to remember.

'You danced, quite marvellously. You leapt over the fire.'

'While that sounds like something I am capable of, I struggle to believe
it.'

'There are a hundred witnesses at my camp. Please, come back. After your
dancing, we lost sight of you. We found your tracks.'

'I was in the deepest desert.'

The prince and the groom shared a glance, a pause. The camel mewed
again.

'Yes, you were. You have been on quite the adventure.'

'And your glove?' Foggo reached into his pack.

'You shook my hand, called me "brother". Then fell over. The glove went
with you.'

'Hmm,' retorted Foggo. He returned the prince's glove. 'Perhaps the
sandsickness got to me earlier than I thought.'

Again, the prince and the groom shared a glance. 'Perhaps.'

'Sandsickness can be terrible,' offered the groom, solicitously.

'Now, admiral, please. Up on my horse. It is an open road back to the
camp.'

'A what?'

'Excuse me, nothing.'

'Ah, very good.' The admiral was back in command. The motion of a horse
under him was much like the sea, and he began to feel better. The camel
at his leg nuzzled him. He did not see his footprints, winding this way
and that, directly adjacent to the wide and well-tended road they
returned to the camp on. He was a man of the sea. What need had he of
the open road?
