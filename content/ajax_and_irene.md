AJAX AND IRENE

*'John said he lost his foreigners, and there's a storm coming.'*

Peter the squire, atop Ajax the horse, rode through the mist-robed
valley. Past pines, ferns and dripping firs, eyed by brown-collared,
long-tailed sheep, they approached the copse where the foreigners had
last been seen. It was easy to find for a native, and native Peter
looked after those in his valley, though he had to be prodded to action
by his wife if they weren't Welsh.

Ajax paused at the edge of the copse, preparing to enter the gloom, and
Peter had to nudge him on with a squeeze. He could see the weak fire the
foreigners had lit. Three horses, not three men, were gathered around it
in a tight ring. The men themselves were sheltering under a tree each,
and each was soaked. Two looked miserable, one waved cheerfully. The
waver advanced, a thickset man with a broken nose and cauliflower ears,
but the hand he offered was dainty. In his other he held a watercolour
tin.

'Hello, sir. I am Dalibor.'

Peter gave the strange name a moment to settle itself in his mind.
'Hello, Dalibor. I'm Peter. I thought you might benefit from a guide
down to the bridge.'

Dalibor looked hesitant, raising his paints in protest, but a second
foreigner stepped forwards. 'I'm Andrej -- please, yes. Down. It is cold
here!'

The final foreigner, taciturn, introduced himself as 'Franc'.

'Very good. Mount up, gentlemen.' The three rode John's horses, and Ajax
knew them. Like school friends, Ajax, Ethiopia, Falstaff and Bishop
rubbed together, bringing Peter a little closer to the others than he
might have chosen. 'So, where have you come from? You aren't prepared
for our summers, I think.'

Dalibor laughed, Andrej shivered, Franc sneezed. 'We're Slovenes.'

'Ah.' At least they weren't French.

'We're having a day trip, sir,' continued Dalibor. 'Industrial
representatives sent from Ljubljana to Cardiff, but we heard of the
beauty of this valley.'

It sounded like labour talk to Peter, and bore the stink of modernity,
but while they were in his valley they were his guests, if not his
friends, and he kept his tongue. Ajax did not, whickering at the heavy
rain.

At the bottom of the valley was the bridge which led to John's, and to
the train station, but the bridge was a carcass now, belly-up in the
river's spate.

'If the bridge is down the road is flooded.' Peter sighed. Guests, not
friends. He had a duty. 'Come on, you can stay with me and the wife. In
the morning, when the weather clears, it will be safe to cross.'

Dalibor was a poor rider. 'This gelding is taking revenge on what I have
for what he lacks,' he muttered to Peter. The squire held back a smile.
Labour talk and crude jokes, in his valley. His father would be turning
in his grave.

The first to meet the four was Peter's young daughter, Mary. She rushed
up and hugged Peter, then stayed with her face pressed into his coat as
fear or shyness overtook her at his strange, shivering companions. Liz
dragged the pair out the way so the Slovenes could reach the fire.

'Thank you.'

'Thanks.'

'*Hvala.'*

But they were not rude guests. Before the first cup of tea was ready,
Franc had befriended their cat, Dragon, Andrej was carefully gathering
coats so they did not drip, and big Dalibor was talking amicably to
little Mary. By the second cup, Dragon was sitting on Franc's lap as he
and Liz ruminated on Wales's and Slovenia's places within the British
and Austro-Hungarian empires, Andrej was settling the horses in the
stable, and Dalibor and Mary were playing a game with paper and pencils.
He even had his watercolours out. By the third cup, even Peter had
grudgingly put down his newspaper and joined them at the fire.

Andrej had Ethiopia, Falstaff and Bishop ready before dawn broke on a
clear day. Liz saw them fed, and Mary shed a tear when they departed.
Peter and Ajax took them to a fording spot upriver, which could now be
safely traversed.

'Goodbye, goodbye.' Dalibor shook his hand. Then he reached into his
pocket, took out a folded note.

'No money, not necessary. You were a guest.'

'We would not insult. For you, my friend.' He pressed it into Peter's
hands, nudged Bishop on, unsteady in his saddle. Franc and Andrej bowed
at the waist and followed their leader. Peter did not open the letter,
but watched the foreigners leave his valley. He was a good host. Once
they were safely beyond his bailiwick, he unfolded the paper. Not a
letter. It was a picture of his daughter, in simple black watercolour.
Dalibor had captured her perfectly. Her crafty eyes, always sparkling at
a trick. Her guileless cheeks, blushing before Peter could fall for it.
Her new white teeth. Somehow Dalibor had even shown her blonde hair with
black paint alone.

'The man was an artist,' Peter said to Ajax.

The next day someone shot an archduke, and all Europe shattered like a
dropped decanter.

Ajax was the valley's first casualty. He enlisted, along with his rider.
He had been spooked by a shell, shied, fell into wire, and was shot.
Horses respond to war the same as men: variously. Ajax had a peaceful
heart. Irene, who replaced him, loved war. She was a violent horse, grey
and muscular and cruel. In Vauclerc she had bitten off the hand of a
child which had tried to stroke her nose. Peter had survived two years
of war on Irene, but he was just as lost as Ajax had been.

From the green soft valley of Gobowen, Peter had come to the hard red
ridge outside Ripont. From the quiet of Wales to the hell of France. No
verdant trees, but soil made at once fertile and rancid by blood. Irene
revelled in the stench, sniffing with quivering wet nostrils, and pawed
at the filth. Peter drew from her mad strength. A sabre quivered in his
hand, with terror and thrill. He and his squadron would smash through
the shelled lines, get among the artillery the Austrians had left out of
position, and cut down the gunners.

Peter's commander, wild-eyed Lancer George, raised his pistol. 'Friends
of England! Be fearless! Kill! Kill! Kill!'

Peter took up the *huzzah,* and Irene's hooves gouged the mud like
shellfire. Grey streak, she overtook Lancer George, overtook them all,
with Peter snatched along. She tore through smoke and iron rain and
thickets of wire, over trenches crowded with woodlouse-grey Austrians.
Peter lashed out with the sabre, catching a foreigner by the face, and
Irene reared in celebration. A grenade exploded and took her eyes and
she fell, horsemeat. Peter was thrown into a shell hole. He lay stunned,
high riding boots in a sludge of dead men.

Red day turned to cold black night. Horses and men wept in the distance.
Two pairs of jackboots crunched towards him, and he was dragged from his
hole by his webbing. The jutting brows of two Austrian helmets crowded
his faint and delirious gaze. Foreign words. An order. One left him, the
other raised his rifle.

Peter spoke no German. '*Moment, s'il vous plaît'.* His executioner
paused and Peter reached into his tunic. A cigarette case, but no
cigarette. Mary\'s portrait. A flare burst overhead, and her
black-blonde hair shone. Like a magic painting, with every horror that
Peter saw, Mary looked more beautiful. He suddenly felt no fear of
death, but an invincible certainty that when the Austrian fired he would
wake up in his valley. The jackboot stamped, an inch from his nose. The
barrel twitched and fired. The bullet, like a million others, sank into
the mud. Peter did not wake up in his valley. The Austrian crouched.

'We are called to attack.' He spoke English. 'A penal battalion for
Slovene *nationalist agitators*.' His voice was bitter, but familiar.
'We will all die.' He put his rifle down, gently, and with slim fingers
took Peter's watch, looked at it for a moment. 'When that is 10 o'clock,
we are all dead. It will be safe to cross, back to your lines.' He
pushed back his helmet, just a little. It showed cauliflower ears, a
broken nose. He had tears in his eyes. His commander barked from the
darkness. Dalibor, unsteady on his feet, left his friend in the mire of
the shell hole and in the glittering light of the flare.

*'One survivor from 62^nd^ squadron, a Second Lieutenant Peter Everett.
Commendation recommended. Austrian counter-attack annihilated, no
prisoners taken.'*
