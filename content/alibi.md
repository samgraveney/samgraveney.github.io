**ALIBI**

\"

I wish to kill you. Don't worry, though. This is a work of fiction. So
it doesn't count as a threat. Look, this story starts with a very clear
and distinct quotation mark. It's speech by a fictional character. It's
a story, which follows the three-act structure. It is not legally
actionable.

Frederick Forsyth wrote a novel, *Dogs Of War*, about a coup in a small
West African country.

The easiest way to realistically convey coup-plotting, Forsyth said, was
to plot a coup. He picked Equatorial Guinea as a real-world equivalent
of the fictional Zangaro. He met mercenaries, understood how to acquire
arms, and issued an order of battle.

A few years after *Dogs of War* was published, Spanish authorities
arrested a band of mercenaries destined for Equatorial Guinea. Forsyth
was never implicated. They could get nothing to stick. It was a
coincidence, Forsyth's lawyers argued. He had merely been researching a
novel.

Forsyth wrote another novel, *Day Of the Jackal,* about killing Charles
De Gaulle. I bring that up because I want to kill you.

Who are you? I have you clearly in mind, in my fictional mind. You're
rich, but you claim to be a man of the people. You spread lies and
mistrust. You try to turn white man on black, man on woman, native
against foreign. I think you are poison, and I want to kill you. But
don't worry, I'm fictional. And as this is fiction, you're probably
fictional too.

Though Charles De Gaulle wasn't fictional.

You know those race riots we had last summer? My brother-in-law is a
black man, family from Cameroon. He had his pelvis smashed by a driver
who veered into him in the unrest. They never caught him. His licence
plate was covered. He had your face on a bumper sticker.

I'm not a violent man. I know there's many ways you might fail. Nature
might take its course. You're old and unhealthy. But nature didn\'t work
fast enough for Hitler, or Mussolini, or Franco, or Dolfuss, Trujillo,
Duvalier, Milosevic, Mobutu, Pinochet.

In this fictional account, I begin my planning. I lack the conviction of
the man who killed Shinzo Abe, shooting point-blank and risking the
death penalty. The men who went to kill Franz Ferdinand took poison and
tried to shoot themselves. I'm no martyr. I want to get away with it.
And I want you to know my every step, so you know how close I get, how
smart I am.

I begin to plan. I install Tails OS on a virgin laptop, the operating
system Edward Snowden used, for my research. My fictional research. My
novel research. I access a different public Wifi network every time I
turn it on. I store the laptop in a locker in the bus station, not in my
home. How will I kill you, then?

Could I bomb you? I probably have the skills to make a bomb, I studied
chemical and mechanical engineering. It's straightforward science,
ammonium nitrate or TATP packed in a capped steel plumbing pipe or empty
fire extinguisher case. But I won't go into too much detail. I wouldn't
want to accidentally reveal bomb-making tips even in a work of fiction,
just like how they had to obscure the meth recipe in *Breaking Bad.* I
could make a bomb, but not one precise enough to kill only you. I don't
want collateral damage.

I'm not indiscriminate.

Could I get a gun? In this fictional account, let's say I am English. I
might not be, and if you're not, don't rest easy. In England guns are
hard to get, though not impossible. I could join a rifle club perfectly
legally. Go shooting every Saturday. Get a safe installed. Become known
to the police as a responsible gun owner. Take your head off with a
single .303. Those were the bullets used to kill Nazis.

I investigate NABIS, the National Ballistics Intelligence Service. I
develop a healthy respect for firearms forensics. Shootings are rare
enough here that the weapon could be identified from the bullet and the
shooter could be tied to the weapon, the suspect pool rendered shallow.

An illegal gun? Enter the Dark Web, travel the Silk Road, pay in Monero
or Zcash crypto. But I'd almost certainly be paying a police officer and
forfeiting my freedom.

I consider poison. Ricin? Anthrax? Anthrax on a handkerchief clapped
around your face like how they got Kim-Jong Nam? I'd be caught, and I'd
die, and everyone would die of anthrax. I research. Markov, a Bulgarian
dissident, was killed in London with a tiny ricin-coated bullet jabbed
into his calf from a needle hidden in an umbrella tip. But I do not have
the scientific nous of the KGB. I turn to nature: the asp viper is the
most poisonous snake in Europe. I could drive to Italy, catch one,
smuggle it back, milk its venom by jamming its fangs through the plastic
lid of a disposable coffee cup. A 96% survival rate when the viper
bites, but I could hypodermic the venom deeper than a snake ever could.
Right into the liver, or the base of the spine. But you'd feel it, you'd
cry out, I'd be caught.

Remember, this is fiction. The above, from bomb to venom, that was the
'fun and games' section of Act 2. I despair, I enter the end of Act 2,
which as any writer knows is the low point, the dark night of the soul.
How will I kill you? Am I to be foiled in my hero's journey?

The idea comes to me as I ride the bus home. I see a tiny woman raising
her car on a ratchet jack. I see a tremendous amount of potential energy
stored in the raised vehicle coming from the woman's thin arms. I see my
solution.

I buy my supplies second- and third-hand. Some I steal from a building
site an hour from my home. A ratchet jack. A drum of half-inch steel
cable, woven from individual steel threads. Hollow steel struts. A heavy
iron fence post. I scar and gouge and notch the post with a hacksaw. I
buy rivets and screws and bolts. I buy two cheap telescopes for their
tripods.

(This is the start of the third act, by the way: Rocky pulls on his
gloves, Batman his cowl. Luke warms up the X-Wing.)

I prototype, assemble, and test my device in a house rented in rural
Wales. It works. I consider the date and location of when I will kill
you, a practicality I had not allowed myself to consider, like
speculating on the contents of Christmas presents in Autumn.

Armistice Day, November 11^th^, will work: you using the triumph over
fascism to market your own English brand, all St. George Crosses and
grinning school cadets who do not understand their role in your
Nuremberg performance. Crucially, your home town's war memorial is
overlooked by husk of a building, a pub which had burnt down in the
riots. You should be careful publishing your itinerary because I know
that at 1100 you will be standing where you stood the last three
Armistice Days. And I know that at 1101, you will be dying.

On November 10^th^ I drive my machine to the pub. I crowbar the door
open. I carry strut and cable and ratchet up to a window. I assemble my
machine. I work the jack, which is connected to a ratchet, to the cable,
which pulls two steel arms concave, ready to snap forward, launching the
notched fence post, guided through a barrel made of steel pipe, out into
space with enough force to penetrate a brick wall. I have made a Roman
ballista.

Using the telescopes' bipods I angle the ballista down at the memorial
and sight along the barrel. At this range, it will shoot true. I wad
cotton wool into the barrel to stop the fence post from sliding out
under its own weight.

I won't stay to watch you die, by the way. I'm not a sadist.

Armistice Day will draw hundreds of false patriots who will flee when
they see your thorax wrecked by my weapon. The notches and gouges will
drag your organs out the exit wound, I didn't want a clean
through-and-through you might survive. No one will be looking for me as
I slip out, wearing a paper poppy on my lapel. And when my machine is
found, there is nothing to tie it to me. No particularly specialist
knowledge was needed to assemble it. No particularly specialist seller
provided any component (I bought the two telescopes in two separate
shops). I have worn gloves and doused everything in bleach. And it's
you, after all. Half the country has motive.

I wait for dawn. I wait for day. It's November, so it's late before it's
light. The crowd gathers. I see you take the stage. The ballista is
sensitive and pre-sighted, so I wait for you to stand in its path. You
will stand in its path. I look at my watch. You will stand in its path
in 60 seconds. You stoop to lay the wreath. You stand up. The bugler
plays *The Last Post* to indicate two minutes' silence. I prepare to
pull the pin which acts as a crude trigger. You put a hand over your
heart. That won't protect it. This is the finale, by the way.

Of course I don't fire. Of course I don't kill you. The Act 3 finale is
an Act 3 twist. After all, how does *Day Of The Jackal* end? I take one
glance around, to make sure I have left no evidence. The two minutes'
silence ends. The crowd begins to disperse moments later. I slip into
it. I leave my ballista. When workers come to renovate the pub, they
will find it. They will call the police. Its purpose and its target will
be quickly deduced. You will be contacted. You will call a press
conference and warn against how political violence rots democracies. You
will say you will never be cowed by threats. You might imply I am a
coward for not taking the shot, or brave for refusing to. I won't care.

In a few months, someone might come across this story. I'll try to get
it published, anonymously, pseudonymously. It's a work of fiction, after
all.

You will see the lengths I went to. You might see that I was merciful,
and spared you, and change your ways. Or you might think I want you to
live in fear, swaddled in cloying security and looking out for a madman
with a needle of asp venom. Regardless, you should take it as a warning.

I just want you to know I'm thinking of you.

"
