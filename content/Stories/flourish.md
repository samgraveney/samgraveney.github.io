Through moist wooden walls, spongy to the touch and permissive to sound,
crept Heleen's quiet, uninsistent, little-girl coughs. It sounded like
she was suppressing them, as if the six-year-old were attentive to her
father's misery. Bart went to glare at her.

There were flowers on little Heleen's bedside table, only daisies, which
Cass must have picked before the sun was up. Cass rose early now. She
rose to get out of their house.

Bart knew Heleen was only pretending to sleep. A polite girl, she would
not cough open-mouthed. Her lips were pressed together and she convulsed
as if drowning. He despised her for her manners, learned in the single
month the Vandervens had retained a fashionable French *au pair*.

He left his liar daughter and moved into their kitchen. It was barren,
not in the fashionable, Puritan manner of their betters, but due to
dilapidation, liquidation and concealment from bailiffs. A table,
stripped of the centrepiece vase, now by Heleen. Lace, clean and mended
by Cass. Earthenware plates dully ignored him, pewter knives pricked. He
had once had silver. It was cold, but there was no fire. Heleen could
not bear the heat.

A knock at his door. He knew it would be Piet Wit, fulfilling a promise
to call after church. An ostentatious Protestant, Wit oozed into the
kitchen like black tar, adjusting rather than removing his hat.

'Vanderven!' he barked. 'Made up your mind?' He began to rummage in his
jacket and Bart braced himself for the irresistible contract and the
seductive pen. But instead Wit flourished the always-mocking news sheet
which charted prices. 'You'll never guess,' laughed Piet. 'They've gone
up!'

'I know.' It wasn't true. Bart hadn't checked, didn't dare, but
still felt the prices' feverish rise. 'Cassie still thinks it's a sin!'
He tried to make it a joke. The Dutch, who had scraped the richest
country in history out of the sea, not allowed to profit by God's bounty
after all their years in the wilderness?

'My sister is pious, Bart. Pious lady. But she's not worldly, is she?'
He sat heavily and Bart resented the wear on the chair's joints.
Resented the mud from the soles of Piet's shining shoes on the flags
Cass had scrubbed. 'Anyway, God is fair. He helps those who help
themselves.' At that moment the despised Heleen coughed again. Both men
paused, and Wit's face rearranged itself into reproach. 'That's my
niece, Bart. I'm worried about her.'

'Almost everything we have is with the insurers. We just need the case
to be closed.'

'They won't pay out, Bart. They never do. And then you go bankrupt-' A
splutter, as if Heleen were collaborating with Wit.

'They might.' Even to Bart's ears this sounded weaker than one of
Heleen's coughs, but the hope was all he had to cling to, like the
sailors who had clung to wrecked spars and drowned spice-nets off the
Antilles. A blasphemous thought. Wit brought out the blasphemer in him.
But those sailors, all fifty of them, had died. Their spars and
spice-nets and faith in God and rescue had not saved them. Perhaps Wit
was right. 'Explain it again, would you?'

The reproach vanished and the smile returned like curtains being thrown
open.

'It's simple. Even Cassandra will understand...'

///

Bart was sitting with Heleen, holding a psalm-sheet on his lap. Wit had
palimpsested it, his black numbers prowling around the Word.

Cass returned with more daisies and two loaves of bread, each a third
spoiled by canal water and bought at half-price. She explained all this
kindly, tiredly, and Bart knew with shame she was touched by the
domestic image, father and sickly daughter. In truth, he had tried to
ignore Heleen, but the kitchen had grown colder and she was burning up
her life like a furnace. He warmed his hands on her forehead.

The couple marched to the kitchen and slipped into *en garde*. Without
preamble or animosity, they resumed their duel.

'Your brother came. I think we have to consider his offer.'

'Piet got lucky and he got rich. Lots didn't. Lot's aren't.

'He showed me everything, all his sums. He didn't get rich on cards or
dice, he followed the markets and made a good decision and he is
inviting us.'

'It's still chance.' She paused, and Bart suspected she was waiting for
Heleen to cough to emphasise her point. 'The house is barren. Your
daughter is consumptive. The storm took your ship. You can't trust to
chance. You need to trust in insurance and industry and thrift and God.'

At the mention of God, Bart slammed his fist on the table and caught the
edge of an earthenware plate. It launched into the air, spinning like a
coin, and landed face down, unbroken. Cass looked from it to Bart with
an expression of desperate triumph, as if this proved something.

'So that's our solution?' he demanded. 'We leave our fate in the hands
of the Good Lord and Master Overvoorde of Maritime Fidelis Credit? How
long might that take? 40 years in the wilderness plus the time it takes
fat Overvoorde to breakfast?'

Cass had, of course, stooped to retrieve the plate and check it for
damage, and placed it in its place on the table set for three, though it
had been months since their girl had left her bed. As she did so, she
muttered, 'the world is mad. Tulips go for more than houses, and my
husband thinks they are a foundation on which to build from ruin.'

'Amsterdam is built on sand,' said Bart, jamming his hat onto his head.
'Which of us really lacks faith?'

Cass watched Bart from the window. At least, she told herself, he had a
psalm-sheet in his hand. Perhaps God would watch over him.
