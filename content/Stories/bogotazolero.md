**NOT ONE STONE UPON ANOTHER WILL BE LEFT**

Word count: 1970

Roa Sierra thought of him as the man with eyes full of destiny. The one
man who recognised that he was the reincarnation of not one but two
great Colombians: Santander *and* Quesada. The eyes full of destiny saw
this fact clearly, and saw that Roa Sierra was needed to serve his
country again.

Jorge Eliecér Gaitan was enjoying his morning. He still practised law,
despite the fact (and this was common knowledge) that the presidency of
Colombia was his for the taking. But he enjoyed his work. He had that
instinct, the fox-sense that let him spot weakness in a story, sentiment
in a juror, sloth in a rival. He would win the election in the year of
the Lord 1948 because he won, consistently and often, and always had.
This did not lead him to indolence. Like a man climbing foothills, his
success merely revealed more peaks to conquer. From the courts to the
world of politics. He would lead Colombia.

The man with eyes full of destiny sat with Roa Sierra on a bench. He had
a newspaper. He always had a newspaper, and his eyes roved over it
unceasingly, as if following an ant across the print. Roa Sierra knew he
was not reading; the eyes did not move a predictable left to right. They
danced, right-left, up-down. They flickered. They coruscated. They saw
everything, had seen everything, would see everything. They had seen the
potential in Roa Sierra. So when the eyes suddenly snapped onto the face
of Roa Sierra, Rosicrucian, Catholic and the reincarnation of both
Santander *and* Quesada, the young Colombian felt no fear, only
excitement.

'Jorge Eliecér Gaitan is an enemy of the Church,' said the man. Roa
Sierra nodded, keen to show he understood. 'We have appealed to his
goodness. We have told him his ideas will be poor with Colombia.' His
*Norteamericano-*accented Spanish was so-so, but Roa Sierra did not hold
it against him. Not everyone could have the eloquence of three
lifetimes. 'And therefore, with sadness, he cannot be allowed to lead
the Colombians into atheism, and therefore damnation. You agree?'

The man thrust the paper at Roa Sierra, who flinched before taking it.
News of Gaitan's popularity. His vast rallies. His threats. If he were
to be harmed, Gaitan had told his baying mob, *not one stone upon
another will be left*. It was a butchering of the gospel of St. Luke,
but that was what Gaitan was: a butcher of the word of God. Gaitan
claimed to be a priest, but was also a lawyer and a politician. Could
they tolerate this Barabbas? *No*, said Santander. *No*, agreed Quesada.
*No*, thought Roa Sierra.

'Yes,' he said. 'I agree with you.'

Gaitan danced down the sunlit Bogota street. He saw only a few faces he
recognised, but no one watching would have known this, for he beamed
benevolence. He, lawyer and socialist, even greeted the policemen and
the soldiers, greeted pinstriped sirs and white-frocked ma'ams. It was
those who would never vote for him that recognised him most easily, and
they shied away with a smile, knowing the Devil was beautiful.

'President!' came a shout. It was Neira, his friend.

Gaitan twisted, hands open at his waist like a dancer, a move cultivated
in the courtroom, making him seem obliging, agile, a twist which
thrilled audiences and spoke of pent-up unspent power.

'Neira!' he replied, loud enough that passers-by would know Gaitan's
list of friends was one name longer.

'We're going for lunch, join us, before you are too busy!'

Gaitan said yes, with uncynical, uncalculating delight.

Roa Sierra was dropped off at his flat while the man with eyes full of
destiny mobilised his scouts to find Gaitan. The young Colombian
unbuttoned his threadbare, ocean-blue jacket and let it pool on the
floor. He walked to his long mirror, inherited from a brother, with the
stride of a king. An observer, he reflected, would have thought him mad.
But it wasn't madness, it was communion. Communing with Santander and
Quesada, communing with his past selves, himselves in one skull, and
gathering old strength for new struggle. He looked deep into his dark
eyes, face perfectly still, startling himself with little gasps as the
muscles of his irises twitched and contracted. In those eyes, just a
little, he saw his destiny. He tried to convince himself that he could
kill if necessary. He had killed when he was Quesada and Santander, he
knew it, the history books said so. But in this weak form of Roa Sierra,
he never had, and he was frightened too, but in the mirror he could set
his face stern and fierce and courageous and heroic. There was a knock
on the door and his eyes flared wide like a spooked horse's, and he
gasped a little louder, as if Santander and Quesada were coming out of
the mirror itself, coming to join him. He turned from the mirror like a
child which has looked too deeply in the dark and has seen something it
did not like, and when he opened the door the man with eyes full of
destiny was firm with him, and he was chastened.

'He's at the *Café Arsenale*.' Roa Sierra took the offered handgun, an
automatic. 'You know how to use it?'

'Yes.'

'Show me.'

Roa Sierra, nervy now as if before an exam, checked the slide and the
ammunition and the hammer. Checked it all. Not the sword of Santander,
but it would have to do. The man with eyes full of destiny nodded.

'Good. Colombia will owe you a debt of much thanks. Now go, please.'

As he paid the bill for the whole table, Gaitan told everyone he had
never laughed as much as at that lunch, when his whole golden future
stretched before him. He might even have been telling the truth. He
drank only a little, and ate lightly. He knew hunger spurred him on like
a predator, like the pumas his grandfather had hunted. He had been
dazzled by the tales of their speed and ferocity. *It's the hungry ones
that live, Jorge, and it's the skulls of the fat cats that're easy to
take.*

Like Moses leading the Israelites, Gaitan had never felt as fit and
sleek and well as when he stepped from *Café Arsenale*. He greeted a nun
by her correct rank. He bowed to a little girl who giggled. And he saw
Roa Sierra, the young man who often came to his office to petition for
work. He nudged the elbows of his companions. 'It's the Rosicrucian!'
and they laughed, not cruelly. Rosicrucians were doing their best.
Gaitan strode forward, his hand outstretched, to Roa Sierra and to the
whole world.

Roa Sierra held the gun unnaturally. The butt was in his right hand with
his left clamped over its top. When he fired, he winced. It hurt his
wrists. But that did not stop him firing again. Gaitan's smile only
faltered when he hit the ground, the impact shaking loose the muscles of
his face which were so accustomed to beaming at voters, juries, and
friends. When the smile disappeared Roa Sierra felt terribly,
irredeemably alone. He knew Gaitan was dead. He did not feel Quesada and
Santander with him. The gun was too heavy, and he dropped it. He was no
conquering hero, he suddenly concluded. He was a boy who had made a
terrible mistake. Clarity rushed into his brain like water into a dry
riverbed. All was black around him save the white blank face of Gaitan
and the puddle of red. And then, in a blinding flash, he saw, beyond
Gaitan's corpse, Gaitan's friends, of whom, until last week, until the
man with eyes full of destiny, he would have considered himself a small
part. He saw passers-by, on the other side of the street stop. And then
he saw them all merge into one Cerberus-creature, many-headed,
many-legged. He saw many mouths open and accusations begin to puke out.
He saw claws raised up on a hundred hands and he saw the skies darken
above him, and he turned on his heel and he ran. He could hear the
Cerberus behind him, galloping, bellowing, belching fire, and he knew he
was in terrible danger. Santander would have slain the beast. Quesada
might have placated it with a poem. But he was neither, mere Roa Sierra,
a man ever alone in the world and suddenly alone in his skull.

Corporal Jimenez heard a terrible shouting. He had heard the shouting of
anger before, many times, outside a house where the husband had drunk
too much, in the stands at the horse races, even from his own wife. But
this had a different texture. Under the anger was misery. Despondence.
Children abandoned, all good gone. And he saw a scrawny boy, eyes wide
and haunted and darting. Life's victim, Jimenez knew by with the
instinct of a merciful man. And then came the crowd. No, not the crowd,
he chastised himself. His sergeant had taught him, there was no *crowd*.
Merely multiple *individuals*. Colombians. His countrymen. And then the
boy was upon him.

'Corporal, please, don't let them kill me!'

And then the tide of Colombians.

'Kill him!' 'He's murdered Gaitan!' 'Get him!' 'Hand him over!'

And Corporal Jimenez saw there was a little blood on the boy's face, and
he smelt the cordite and the sweat, and he knew the boy had done
something terrible. But Corporal Jimenez had no killer instinct. His
role was to protect, to see justice done. It sounded high-minded, and he
was teased for philosophising at the barracks, but he was not a clever
man, and so he splinted his brain with the law, and others mistook
kindness for cleverness. He placed himself between the boy and his
countrymen. He raised his authoritative hand. It was grabbed at.

'Watch it!' he ordered. 'Everyone calm down!'

'He killed Gaitan! We'll kill him!'

His countrymen pressed in on him and he drew his truncheon. Even now he
could not strike Colombian faces but swung at Colombian shins. It was
not enough. The crowd seized the boy who screamed like a rat. Jimenez
drew his revolver and fired into the air, and his countrymen receded. He
grabbed the boy by the back of the neck and dragged him blindly out of
the morass of Colombians and together they staggered into a pharmacy.

'Lock the door, sir,' ordered Jimenez to the startled owner, and
brandished his revolver at his countrymen, heart thundering. The door
clicked shut and the owner saw the blood on the boy's face.

'What did you do?'

All the boy could stammer was 'Oh Lord, powerful things! Oh Virgin of
Carmen, save me!'

An hour passed, and Jimenez, despairingly, saw more and more of his
countrymen fill the street. He wanted to summon aid but the pharmacy
lacked a telephone. There was a flicker of hope when the street cleared
a little, and he prayed for the grey uniforms of his comrades to march
through and save him and the boy, still praying on the floor, staring
into a shaving mirror the owner had given him. But no police came.
Instead, Jimenez saw a motor-car. He waved his revolver, then pointed
it, but the driver, in goggles, teeth bared, ignored these warnings.

The motor-car smashed the window. Jimenez, man of mercy, could not fire
as the first of his countrymen came through the glass. He fell under
their feet, truncheon in hand, but he was helped up then restrained like
a drunk who fought to return to wine. They took the boy.

Jimenez learned later they crushed his skull with a rock, and then they
had burnt down Bogota, and he could not understand any of it.
