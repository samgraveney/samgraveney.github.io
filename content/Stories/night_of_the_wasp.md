Title: Night Of The Wasps
Slug: night-of-the-wasps
Date: 2026-01-01
Category: short-stories
Summary: short-stories

One year, when I was a child, wasps built a nest in the kitchen-wall
vent. At first, I liked it. It blocked dad's smoke; he would stand
outside the kitchen and enjoy a cigarette and, lordly, admire his little
house and his little family.

The nest was a bulbous, papery thing. It hummed in the summer, quietened
in a cold autumn and revived in winter as, Christmas luxury, we were
allowed the heating for two hours daily. Black-and-yellow devils came
creeping in. One stung the dog and the dog screamed and ran into a wall.
It split its scalp. The vet's bill hurt. The wasps had to die.

But it was Christmas: exterminators were on holiday or charging holiday
rates. We were poor. We were economising. Dad said he'd stopped smoking.
So we wore long sleeves and hats and kept the kitchen cold. Our
stitched-up dog hunted all day. It learnt to smash with paws, not to
bite, after its black gums swelled with wasp poison. Dad, angry and
cold-turkey, prowled with a badminton racquet. Mum cooked in her wedding
veil and long bridal gloves, ghost of Christmas Past.

Christmas Eve: we put on our best clothes and walked to Midnight Mass.
Mum used powder to cover her wasp stings. Dad lagged behind, breath
fogging like cigarette smoke.

It was just the local church, a squat building made of stone always wet
to the touch, but the glow of candlelight and the late hour gave it all
a sense of barbarous pre-Roman power. It was cool inside, and everyone
kept their coats on, adding to the pagan air. We shared a pew with
another family, and the wood was icy through my best trousers. I prayed
my own prayers, not following the priest's script. I begged God's help
against the wasps. That they were also his creatures never occurred to
me.

When the service ended, there was coffee in the too-hot hall and I
complained I was tired and left mum and dad to talk to their church
friends who had Old Testament names and children who did not go to my
school.

I walked home by moonlight. In my memory, every Christmas Eve was lit
bright as the inside of a silver bowl. In the cold air I could see a
hundred miles. I had an ink-black clear-edged moon shadow. I stood alone
in front of my house. Midnight, and all neighbours asleep or at church.

From my coat sleeve I pulled a rubber tube, stolen from chemistry class.
From behind a plantpot, a baking tray. With panoply I approached our car
to siphon petrol. Fuel cap off, tube in, suck. My lips began to ache,
and the fumes made my heard spin. The fuel climbed the hose slow like
treacle. Then it was in my mouth, an awful, adult smell suddenly turned
to flavour. Retching, I filled the tray.

Moving the fuel was difficult. It sloshed left and right with each step
like a live thing trying to escape. The kitchen vent was at the house's
rear, the wasp nest bulging like a tumour.

I knew the fumes which made me woozy were lethal to the wasps. Their
little lungs would fill and they would die. I raised the fuel-filled
tray; I was short, the nest high, the effort made me grunt and petrol
ran down my wrists and under my shirt and tickled my hairless armpits.
The wasps, even in dead winter, sensed danger. They sallied, a
black-yellow foam. Some were struck down by fumes and I could hear them
drop into the tray with the hiss of a struck match. Others, valiant in
the cold, explored my wrists and hair. I had to be brave; dropping the
tray would raise a clatter and the neighbours would comment and the fuel
would stain. A wasp stung my scalp, another landed on my shoulder, and
nestled in the rucked fabric where the seam of my good coat accommodated
my Atlas pose. The match-hisses intensified. My strength was failing,
the tray impossibly heavy, and, unable to see, unwilling to look up, I
imagined a towering pile of wasps above me like old photos of bison
skulls from the American prairie. My arms shook, and fuel lapped out and
wetted my stung head. With the sort of groan I imagined Samson would
have given, I rose to tiptoes, calves bursting, triceps twanging like
violin strings about to snap, and pressed the tray point-blank into the
nest, swamping it. It muffled the hum. I held it there for ten more
seconds, and the hum stopped. There was luxuriant pain as I lowered my
burden, circulation returning to aching fingers.

I left dying wasps behind like I was Yperite and returned to the car,
clumsily refilling it with petrol and black-yellow bodies. Inside the
house I stripped, dumped my clothes in a bin bag, tied the neck and
stuffed it under my bed (by the time mum noticed them missing, I would
be hero, wasp-vanquisher). I showered and scrubbed the fuel stink from
myself and the tray and emerged pink, with a swollen skull from the
sting.

In bed I kicked my legs with sombre childish contemplation, proud to
have slaughtered a hecatomb for parents whose emotional depth I was only
beginning to grope at but whose pain I sensed and sought to salve. Then
I slept.

I was carried, half-asleep and coughing, from bed to car. My memories
are fragmented by confusion, sleep and cold, but I remember the house,
now lit flickering orange, now siren-blue, now nacreous silver by the
moon which watched all impassively and made a shimmer of the firemen's
hoses.

We stood outside our burned house on Christmas morning. I followed dad
behind it and watched him pocketing wasp corpses where the nest had been
and where a fireman said the blaze had begun. Later, fearing blame, I
searched dad's pockets: no wasps, but cigarette butts, and a spent
match.
