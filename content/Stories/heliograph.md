HELIOGRAPH

Night. The screams were clearest at night. Gobbled up at Troy, borne on
the southern wind, they rained over Philoctetes, king, cripple, maroon.

Nine years of exile on Lemnos left him a withered reflection of the king
he had been. His kingdom collapsed to the foot of a stubborn apple tree
slouching over a gritty beach. His kingly wealth only salt-eaten
blankets and a rusted sword, a shimmering bronze shield, wrist-thick
arrows and a bow taller than he was. Funerary gifts for a living corpse.

Dawn. Philoctetes shifted on the coarse sand with a grunt, and woke the
flies which then, rejoicing, bounced on apple cores and fish guts. His
bony archer's shoulders ached, lopsided from the draw, but worse were
his legs, blackened and skeletal and suppurating. Hera had ripped them,
never to heal, only to stink, forcing his comrades to cast him away

He no longer had the strength to drag himself to the nearby stream,
instead he licked the dew from his shield like a thirsty dog. Then he
took up a handful of sand, and began to scrub at the bronze. He
burnished it to a mirror, but, gorgon-like, avoided his reflection. He
had been renowned for his beauty, a credible suitor for sweet Helen. He
did not want to know what had become of his face. He knew what had
happened to his soul.

The sun was up, and with it the army of flies formed ranks and marched
up his back, his face, his weeping legs. They stung, a thousand tiny
swordsmen attacking their own Troy. He failed to catch one, and raised
his shield to his lap.

Every day, all day, he used his warrior's shield to betray his
countrymen. He knew, god-ordained and oath-bound, the Greeks would not
win their war if he were rescued. But the heat, the thirst, the flies,
the stench, the deformation and the degradation and the loneliness which
pummelled him like heavy waves, all were too much. He forlornly
reflected the bitter sun from Lemnos out into the Aegean Sea, hoping his
signal would be seen by the lowest fisherman, and that he would be
saved, be healed.

Paris haunted him. He would saunter along the beach, admiring his
beauty, reflected in his golden apple, raising his eyes only to smirk at
Philoctetes' desolation. Odysseus would visit too, the liar king, who
had lured Philoctetes to Lemnos with the promise of a cure. The author
of the oath they had all sworn, he had pinned Philoctetes in place on a
spike of honour. And sometimes Philoctetes' old friend Heracles
descended and comforted him, but a single breath of cold wind was all it
would take to drive off the god and leave the man alone, save his sanity
and hunger.

Midday. He dropped his shield and took up Heracles' bow. After nine
years of salt, it was still supple. The string was made of Philoctetes'
own braided hair, greased by Philoctetes' own sweat. The
straightest-shooting bow in the world, and the greatest war in man's
history happening over the horizon's curve. He nocked an arrow, flexed
and re-flexed the bow, drew the string loosely, and shot an apple from
the tree. He ate, and his legs ached, and his stomach ached, and flies
bunched in the corners of his eyes.

He heaved the shield again. Scythe-like, he swept the beam east and
west. His failing vision saw things out at sea, fish or monsters or
boats, but all ignored him. The metal was hot to the touch but his hands
were now numb leather.

Dusk. A tremor of pain ran from toe to shoulder and he spasmed, dropping
the shield. It did not roll far, and the bronze of its face was azure as
the evening light. Philoctetes covered his eyes as he reached for it. He
could not face himself.

He wept when the sun dipped over Greece. The dying red rays, low on the
water, could only be reflected into the sky, and the gods already knew
where he was. The flies disappeared. The sand stopped baking him. The
tide rose and he could run his fingers in the cooling water. The tree
danced in the breeze and occasionally an apple would drop with a rich
thud. Freed from physical hurt, he wound himself in shame like a shroud.

Night. He, king, had sworn an oath to serve Menelaos in his war against
Troy. He could not fight, but he still kept that oath in exile. His
suffering sated Hera, reflecting her wrath from all Greeks onto one.
That was valour, surely? A man against a goddess? Yet he had spent the
entire day trying to save himself. It was mere bad luck, not honour,
that stopped him from breaking his bond. Heracles had taken the heavens
on his shoulders for Atlas, but Philoctetes shied at a far lighter
burden. He did not deserve the bow. This was his ache every night.
Snatches of sleep, claws of misery, and a chorus of sea-borne screams.
The shield glowed like bone under the moonlight.

Dreams would come, of course. Reflections of a life, echoing in a head
full as his stomach was empty. His first fight, kiss, hunt, kill.
Coronation, gifts, banquets. Helen, then an ill-remembered wife. His
life stretched away like it was glimpsed between two mirrors, bouncing
into darkness, irretrievable.

Dawn. The sun's red face watched him, like a lion at its prey. At its
first hot breaths, Philoctetes' courage evaporated with the dew and he
took up a handful of sand, and began to burnish with the desperation of
a man bailing. He could not endure another day, oath be damned. Not
again, not again.

His watch resumed, his heliograph shining across the sea. The sun
climbed higher, towards the dangerous angle where, in his shield, held
near horizontal, he might catch a glimpse of his ruined face. Hunger
roared, and he dropped the shield to transfix a leaping silver fish,
reflecting the sky and the water in its scales, with a dark arrow tied
to a cable of hair and dried fish-skin. He reeled it in, and ate it raw.
He could have fried it on the shield, but he needed it to glint.

Midday. Vigil. He sweated and thirsted. The sand under his body cooked
him. A dark shape scudded across the water, and his beam ran along it
like the hand of an unwanted lover. The sails luffed, the ship changed
course, and Philoctetes realised with dread that he was to be rescued.
It was a bireme, a warship. Dozens of rows of oars, like the legs of a
black crab, emerged from its belly and it crawled towards him. Two blue
eyes were painted on its prow. Scrutinised, he snatched the shield to
his groin to cover his shame. Trojans? Had they won? Or Greeks, angry
that he was breaking his oath, frightened of Hera?

Odysseus shaded his eyes against the bronze glimmer on the shoreline.
Eleven years ago he had respected Philoctetes. Ten years ago, they had
sailed for Troy together. Nine years ago, his friend's stench in his
nostrils, and knowing this meant a god's ire, he had tricked him and
marooned him. And now he needed him. The ship crunched onto the sand.

'You can put that shield down, Philoctetes. We're here as friends.'

First, the animal need for revenge. Philoctetes took up Heracles' bow.
No rescue could make up for nine years of strife, a fate clever Odysseus
had dodged. Arrow, nocked, loosed. A perfect shot, cutting towards the
bridge of the Ithacan's nose. Diomedes, big Diomedes, golden Diomedes,
caught it from the air like swatting a fly.

'We're here for you, Philoctetes.'

His reply was another arrow, which Diomedes took on his shield. The two
heroes jumped from the bireme, followed by a dozen men. A third arrow
took a bearded soldier in the thigh, and he went down in the surf,
shrieking. A fourth found a hand. The Greeks locked shields, and
advanced. A fifth arrow bounced into the air, a sixth snapped on a
greave. Grunting in unison, they advanced. Arrow after impotent arrow
tore from the bow of the wounded king. Finally, his mouth opened, and he
screamed. Hate for Odysseus, for his tricks. Hate for himself, for
seeking safety over honour, for being so pitiable that Greek pathos
would save him, and the oath to Menelaos would shatter. The Greeks would
be routed, and Paris would triumph. And then all men would see
Philoctetes for the creature he had become, the creature he was suddenly
confronted by, reflected in a dozen polished shields. His dark eyes were
pale now, like linen bleached in the sun. His kind mouth a yawning and
toothless cave, nose running, a matted beard. He saw his blackened legs
trailing behind him, a gorgon's snake tail. there was no man left, just
a dozen irredeemable creatures snarling. A ruin, he could not fight them
off. He could not let himself be saved. He had no choice. He closed his
eyes, dropped his bow, groped for words.

'Just kill me. Don't let them say I broke my oath.'

Diomedes picked him up effortlessly, and carried him to the ship. Over
his shoulder, the wounded king saw Odysseus collecting Heracles' bow. He
saw him spin the shield into the surf, light bouncing wildly, then
snuffed out forever.

'Your oath is better served in Ilium, Philoctetes.' The king felt the
hero's voice reverberate, chest to chest.

The ship was dragged back into the water. Philoctetes, wrapped in
blankets and given wine, watched Lemnos recede. His apple tree, his
blanket, his home of nine years. Golden sand, a sea breeze, the flies
and the fish for company. The ship trudged east by oar, into the
screaming wind.
