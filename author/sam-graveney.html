Ledrblaka

LEDRBLAKA

The big man stood in the twilit surf, facing the family and panting. They had not seen him approach; they had been whelking. He had come from the sea, but there was no boat. In one hand he held a hatchet, in the other, a dagger. He did not move.

Father drew himself to his full height, and pulled his knife from his belt, a mere tool, no weapon. Son looked about, scrabbling in the cold, wet sand, and found a smooth pebble, the right size for his hand. He stood a little behind Father. Mother clutched toddling Daughter.

Father levelled the knife, twisting his body to present a narrower target, dredging up what training he remembered from the war. The man with the axe and the dagger still had not moved, his breathing slowing, his face neutral.

Father asked what the man wanted, said they had nothing worth taking. No answer came.

The man took a step forward, and another, out of the surf. Big, heavy steps, knife and axe swinging limply at his sides. Spray hit Father in the face, and he retreated, knocking into Son. They both fell. Mother darted forward and pulled Father to his feet with her thin arms and hysterical strength. She was trembling to the point of vibrating. Daughter was crouched on the sand, 'being little,' as Mother had taught her when violence came from the sea.

The man with axe and dagger stumbled forwards again. For all his violent motion his expression had not changed from its dead neutrality. Though he faced them, he barely seemed aware of them.

Son lurched to his feet, had backed away, had wound his arm back almost behind his head, and had loosed the pebble. It struck the man beneath his vacant right eye. As it struck, the man opened his mouth, and the bone-noise of stone on skull came out from the void, the closest he had come to speech. Blood welled and ran out of him too fast as it diluted in the seawater on his face and raced down his neck and under his ragged shirt.

For a moment, it looked as if the blood was retreating, back up, back into the cut, as a shadow rose from his throat to his chin. Not shadow, but short dun hair. The man's face furred like mould on meat. The gash, horizontal, under his eye, stretched and split as the man's face elongated. His nose shrank, flattened and blackened. The eyes separated and darted to either side of the long face. His ears climbed on top of his head and stretched for Heaven.

Though he kept a man's body, the head was a man-sized rabbit's. The fur darkened under the side-facing eye as the gash kept bleeding. The mouth flapped open and big rabbit teeth gnashed. The ears pivoted about wildly. Mother groaned. Father cursed, then the curse turned into a mutter of horror as the rabbit-man stepped forward. Father extended his knife, meekly warding, not striking, but it continued, and the knife sank into its belly. It stopped and slowly turned its head to so one big, watery, blank eye was fixed on Father.

Again, its face changed. Again, its head moved to keep Father fixed in sight as the eyes moved across the rabbit skull like bubbles under ice. The fur receded, leaving black mottled skin, and the rabbit's snout became half-human, half-pig. The big ears turned forwards, became bald and fleshy and ribbed like a longship's hull. The mouth widened into a grin, and when it opened a hundred needle teeth flashed. From the mouth came a terrible sound that only Son and Daughter could hear, a screeching they recognised, but had never heard so loud. The man had the head of a bat.

Daughter had liked bats, she had liked that they were allowed to stay up later than her, and loved the moonlight. This bat seemed to love nothing, was aware of nothing. She screamed and screamed and screamed.

Mother shouted at Father. His hand was still on the knife handle which was still in the creature's stomach. He was gazing at the bat with dread. But he heard her shout, and he heard Daughter\'s shriek, and he acted. He pulled the knife back, then slid it slowly and firmly under the creature's chin.

The knife was too short and too blunt to pierce the skull, but the damage was done. The short axe and the long dagger dropped from the creature's hands, never used, never even brandished. Blood drizzled from its throat down Father's forearm. And the face changed one last time. The snout became longer than ever, with a big, wide leathery tip. The eyes grew, and long eyelashes surrounded them. The fur returned, coarser than the rabbit's. A strip of longer hair sprouted between tall ears. It had become a donkey. Its soft lips slobbered over the father's arm, seeking its own blood. It died silently and without complaint.

As the creature collapsed, it disappeared, leaving only the crumple of its clothes, which bobbed in the surf. Father looked at the blood on his blade, and the blood which had run down his arm and, at the crook of his elbow, dropped onto his feet. He squatted to wash his hands in the sea, suddenly clenching his eyes shut and gasping and trembling. Son moved forwards and picked up the dead creature's shirt, holding it by the shoulders to investigate.

A fleshy lump fell out from the shirt and splatted into the wet sand between dagger and axe. The boy crouched, and lifted it up, holding it by the tips of its wings. Dead, the evening breeze made it jerk. Rabbit teeth forced their way from the small bat mouth. Black bat legs terminated in hooves. And the frames which held its wings taut sprouted ten white human fingernails at their tips.

Admiral Foggo In The Desert

ADMIRAL FOGGO IN THE DESERT

Admiral Foggo woke up in a pile of sand. He had a terrific headache and felt nauseous. He was lost in a desert, and so concluded he had a condition called sandsickness which, though fictional, affected him badly.

He stood up carefully and slithered down the dune a little way. He sucked his teeth and rotated on the spot. Nothing but yellow sand and blue sky and the sun, high up.

'It's damn hot,' he said to himself. He wiped his sweating brow on his navy woollen Navy jacket and looked down at his navy Navy trousers and his black Navy shoes. He was bareheaded. The prince's men must have taken his white Navy hat. The sandsickness was intolerable.

'I believe it is because I am so used to the motion of the sea, I must have inverted sea legs.' Admiral Foggo, a scientific man, hypothesised, and then tested. 'I will have to walk to safety. My sandsickness is psychosomatic, a consequence of my life on the sea. Therefore, I must fool my own brain.' He pictured the lubbers he had known, who had gone green even at the sight of his first command, the sleek and lethal white cutter named Happy Pig. He imitated their wide-splayed posture, hands held out for balance, and took a couple of rolling steps. He felt the sandsickness abate a little, and looked about again. On ships, sometimes things appear over the horizon without much effort on one's own part. But he had not moved. He did not know where to move to.

'\"If one does not know to which port one is sailing, no wind is favorable". Seneca.' This was addressed to himself, not Seneca, who had been dead for 1,965 years. But he was fond of naval aphorisms. Foggo could tell north and south and up and down by the stars. He could tell Pacific from Atlantic only by the merest glance at chart and compass. But cast into the desert by the wrathful prince, he was all at sea.

'The prince is an Arab. Therefore it is likely I am in Arabia, as that is where I was with him recently. I am familiar with the Red Sea and the Persian Gulf. They tend to be south, west and east of most of Arabia. I must merely not walk north, and I shall reach a coast. No doubt I can commandeer a fisherboat and be on my way. So, let me find north, and walk from it. This repulsive desert shall be my open road, and carry me to the sea. By my lack of shadow, it is either night or midday. By the presence of the sun, midday. Its verticality also suggests I am near the equator, so I am indeed likely in Arabia.' It all fell into place. 'I will wait until it dips, for that shall be west.' He paused. 'I am sure the sun sets in the west even in Arabia. I would have heard otherwise.'

He sat back down and fell asleep, and woke in the blackest night. There was no hint of the sun at any point on the horizon.

'Ah. An error on my part.' He glanced about, making sure no one had seen him, but no one had, because he was lost in the desert. 'The North Star will do.' He located it easily, squashed between his friends the Little and Large Dippers. He turned his back to it and began to walk, and then tripped over his rucksack.

'Aha. Jetsam.' He discovered rum, half-drained, likely by villains, and a white glove. He shouldered his burden, turned his back to the North Star, and weighed anchor.

He made it fifty feet before he was bitten by a snake. He sighed. He had taken a bullet at the Battle of Santiago de Cuba, from one of his own enlisted. A snake bite was much less serious. He picked up the snake by its head and looked at it with disappointment. 'You'll regret that, serpent. My blood is more salt than iron after a lifetime on the seas. You're in for a thirsty night.' He dropped it, and it slithered away, south.

'The snake shall mark my road. It will carry a bit of me ahead of me.' Now snakesick and still a little sandsick, he advanced. After many hours of following the snake road, the sun rose on his left. Admiral Foggo called out to the snake, 'you see? I can navigate just as well here as on the seas.'

The snake ignored him. It wound sideways, which Foggo ascribed to it being salt-drunk. 'I bet you've never even seen the sea.'

He remembered saying something similar to the prince. That damn prince! All Foggo had done was praise the sea and criticise the desert. And for that, he had been exiled to the sands. He frowned and reached for the white glove.

'It's the prince's glove!' The snake was still not interested. 'I must have snatched it in exchange for my white Navy hat.' He trudged on, in companionable silence with the snake, for many hours.

'It's damn hot. Must be midday.' He paused to sip a little rum. When he looked up, the snake had gone, burrowing for cooler sand. 'Ah, you coward.'

He pressed on. The snakesickness went, and he saw a cactus that was shaped like a fat man holding a cigar in a wide hand. A small brown bundle was at the fat man's feet. Foggo approached it.

'What are you? Some sort of desert seal?' The baby camel squinted at him warily. 'Aha, I know you're a camel. Are you sandsick too?'. There was no response. 'Have some rum.'

He fed the young camel from the bottle of rum, and it mewed. He picked it up and gave it a close look. 'I suppose you want to come with me.' Admiral Foggo was taking command of the enlisted. Naval life was not solitary, and he was unused to being alone. The camel's long neck draped over his shoulder. It was heavy. But he had someone to talk to on the desert road.

'I don't know why the prince objected. This desert is dreadful.'

The camel's silence was a rebuke.

'I could have been more polite, I know.'

It coughed.

'He did invite me to his home. But why invite a naval man if you don't want to hear about the sea?'

It shivered.

'You can't be cold, sailor. It is an oven out here. Not like the open ocean. The sea breeze! This place is a land lacking the thinnest stream.'

He advanced, waddle-like, relentless as the waves. Admiral Foggo would not be kept from the water. He would march from the desert. The camel squirmed, and he put it down and gave it more rum, medicinally. He had some too, medicinally. They walked together, until Foggo paused, seeing something sausage-shaped in the sand.

'Is that a desert seal?' The camel didn't know, so they walked towards it. It was another camel, big and dead. Foggo knew it was the little camel's mother. They looked alike.

'Condolences,' he said. When the sailor who had shot him had visited him in the Happy Pig's hospital, they had discussed naval strategy to avoid recrimination. He tried the same thing with the camel.

'The prince invited me here to discuss whether naval theory could be applied to desert warfare.'

The camel did not have a strong opinion on this, or was distracted, or grieving, and Admiral Foggo knew when to be quiet. In the silence which followed, as they walked through the desert, on the clear wind Foggo's ears detected hoofbeats.

'Ahoy,' he muttered, slowly, eyes following where ears pointed. Stuffing the camel down the front of his woolen jacket, he crept to the top of a dune. A pack of horsemen, advancing. Their steeds were sweating, eyes wide and wild, foaming at the mouth. Their riders were swathed in cloth, faces obscured, but swords and lances and rifles adorned high saddles.

'A war party! No doubt come to finish me off!' Foggo dropped to his belly, squashing the camel, which mewed.

The mew carried. It was piercing, like a ship's whistle. There was a command in Arabic, and a horse reared, slowed, stopped, turned. A turbaned head with green flashing eyes turned, hands pulling at the reins. One was gloved in white. The other was bare.

'The prince! You damn fool, you've killed us both!' Foggo was accustomed to collective punishment, as when a ship goes down the sea takes no prisoners. He assumed the camel would perish too. Then he remembered where he was. He plucked the creature from his jacket and placed it down on its unsteady, foal-like legs.

'Run! Save yourself!'

The camel took two steps and tumbled.

'Goddamn this blasted sandsickness!' Foggo rose. The prince saw him, and waved his lance. The horsemen circled, and began bearing down on him. Foggo scooped up the camel and began to run, the sandsickness driving him to his knees again and again. The sun bleached all strength from him. The sand sucked at his feet. The dry air tore at his throat, and the hoofbeats grew louder. A shadow fell over him, a rider on a horse.

'Admiral, are you feeling quite alright? You had us worried!'

Foggo staggered, fell, clutching his four-legged crewmate to his heart.

'Strike! Kill me but spare my crew!' He knelt up, eyes shut, awaiting the fatal blow.

'Admiral, please! You're quite delirious.' He opened one eye. The prince was standing before him, dismounted. His horse was breathing a little heavily, but otherwise was demure. One of the prince's grooms caught up with them and fed it an apple. 'You have had us terribly worried.'

'Eh?'

The prince gave a command in Arabic, and the groom gently took the young camel, gave it water, and placed it in a saddlebag.

'Last night - I must say, I have never seen a man drink as much as you did. You were quite lively.'

'We fought, did we not?'

The prince helped him to his feet.

'Only with words. The merits of the sea and the desert. You have a poet's heart. My youngest son, I am afraid, you convinced to become a sailor with your descriptions. He is already on the train to Alexandria. You wrote him a letter of recommendation to the British attache.'

'And then what did I do?' asked Foggo, eyes narrowing. Perhaps he was beginning to remember.

'You danced, quite marvellously. You leapt over the fire.'

'While that sounds like something I am capable of, I struggle to believe it.'

'There are a hundred witnesses at my camp. Please, come back. After your dancing, we lost sight of you. We found your tracks.'

'I was in the deepest desert.'

The prince and the groom shared a glance, a pause. The camel mewed again.

'Yes, you were. You have been on quite the adventure.'

'And your glove?' Foggo reached into his pack.

'You shook my hand, called me "brother". Then fell over. The glove went with you.'

'Hmm,' retorted Foggo. He returned the prince's glove. 'Perhaps the sandsickness got to me earlier than I thought.'

Again, the prince and the groom shared a glance. 'Perhaps.'

'Sandsickness can be terrible,' offered the groom, solicitously.

'Now, admiral, please. Up on my horse. It is an open road back to the camp.'

'A what?'

'Excuse me, nothing.'

'Ah, very good.' The admiral was back in command. The motion of a horse under him was much like the sea, and he began to feel better. The camel at his leg nuzzled him. He did not see his footprints, winding this way and that, directly adjacent to the wide and well-tended road they returned to the camp on. He was a man of the sea. What need had he of the open road?

Ajax and Irene

AJAX AND IRENE

'John said he lost his foreigners, and there's a storm coming.'

Peter the squire, atop Ajax the horse, rode through the mist-robed valley. Past pines, ferns and dripping firs, eyed by brown-collared, long-tailed sheep, they approached the copse where the foreigners had last been seen. It was easy to find for a native, and native Peter looked after those in his valley, though he had to be prodded to action by his wife if they weren't Welsh.

Ajax paused at the edge of the copse, preparing to enter the gloom, and Peter had to nudge him on with a squeeze. He could see the weak fire the foreigners had lit. Three horses, not three men, were gathered around it in a tight ring. The men themselves were sheltering under a tree each, and each was soaked. Two looked miserable, one waved cheerfully. The waver advanced, a thickset man with a broken nose and cauliflower ears, but the hand he offered was dainty. In his other he held a watercolour tin.

'Hello, sir. I am Dalibor.'

Peter gave the strange name a moment to settle itself in his mind. 'Hello, Dalibor. I'm Peter. I thought you might benefit from a guide down to the bridge.'

Dalibor looked hesitant, raising his paints in protest, but a second foreigner stepped forwards. 'I'm Andrej -- please, yes. Down. It is cold here!'

The final foreigner, taciturn, introduced himself as 'Franc'.

'Very good. Mount up, gentlemen.' The three rode John's horses, and Ajax knew them. Like school friends, Ajax, Ethiopia, Falstaff and Bishop rubbed together, bringing Peter a little closer to the others than he might have chosen. 'So, where have you come from? You aren't prepared for our summers, I think.'

Dalibor laughed, Andrej shivered, Franc sneezed. 'We're Slovenes.'

'Ah.' At least they weren't French.

'We're having a day trip, sir,' continued Dalibor. 'Industrial representatives sent from Ljubljana to Cardiff, but we heard of the beauty of this valley.'

It sounded like labour talk to Peter, and bore the stink of modernity, but while they were in his valley they were his guests, if not his friends, and he kept his tongue. Ajax did not, whickering at the heavy rain.

At the bottom of the valley was the bridge which led to John's, and to the train station, but the bridge was a carcass now, belly-up in the river's spate.

'If the bridge is down the road is flooded.' Peter sighed. Guests, not friends. He had a duty. 'Come on, you can stay with me and the wife. In the morning, when the weather clears, it will be safe to cross.'

Dalibor was a poor rider. 'This gelding is taking revenge on what I have for what he lacks,' he muttered to Peter. The squire held back a smile. Labour talk and crude jokes, in his valley. His father would be turning in his grave.

The first to meet the four was Peter's young daughter, Mary. She rushed up and hugged Peter, then stayed with her face pressed into his coat as fear or shyness overtook her at his strange, shivering companions. Liz dragged the pair out the way so the Slovenes could reach the fire.

'Thank you.'

'Thanks.'

'Hvala.'

But they were not rude guests. Before the first cup of tea was ready, Franc had befriended their cat, Dragon, Andrej was carefully gathering coats so they did not drip, and big Dalibor was talking amicably to little Mary. By the second cup, Dragon was sitting on Franc's lap as he and Liz ruminated on Wales's and Slovenia's places within the British and Austro-Hungarian empires, Andrej was settling the horses in the stable, and Dalibor and Mary were playing a game with paper and pencils. He even had his watercolours out. By the third cup, even Peter had grudgingly put down his newspaper and joined them at the fire.

Andrej had Ethiopia, Falstaff and Bishop ready before dawn broke on a clear day. Liz saw them fed, and Mary shed a tear when they departed. Peter and Ajax took them to a fording spot upriver, which could now be safely traversed.

'Goodbye, goodbye.' Dalibor shook his hand. Then he reached into his pocket, took out a folded note.

'No money, not necessary. You were a guest.'

'We would not insult. For you, my friend.' He pressed it into Peter's hands, nudged Bishop on, unsteady in his saddle. Franc and Andrej bowed at the waist and followed their leader. Peter did not open the letter, but watched the foreigners leave his valley. He was a good host. Once they were safely beyond his bailiwick, he unfolded the paper. Not a letter. It was a picture of his daughter, in simple black watercolour. Dalibor had captured her perfectly. Her crafty eyes, always sparkling at a trick. Her guileless cheeks, blushing before Peter could fall for it. Her new white teeth. Somehow Dalibor had even shown her blonde hair with black paint alone.

'The man was an artist,' Peter said to Ajax.

The next day someone shot an archduke, and all Europe shattered like a dropped decanter.

Ajax was the valley's first casualty. He enlisted, along with his rider. He had been spooked by a shell, shied, fell into wire, and was shot. Horses respond to war the same as men: variously. Ajax had a peaceful heart. Irene, who replaced him, loved war. She was a violent horse, grey and muscular and cruel. In Vauclerc she had bitten off the hand of a child which had tried to stroke her nose. Peter had survived two years of war on Irene, but he was just as lost as Ajax had been.

From the green soft valley of Gobowen, Peter had come to the hard red ridge outside Ripont. From the quiet of Wales to the hell of France. No verdant trees, but soil made at once fertile and rancid by blood. Irene revelled in the stench, sniffing with quivering wet nostrils, and pawed at the filth. Peter drew from her mad strength. A sabre quivered in his hand, with terror and thrill. He and his squadron would smash through the shelled lines, get among the artillery the Austrians had left out of position, and cut down the gunners.

Peter's commander, wild-eyed Lancer George, raised his pistol. 'Friends of England! Be fearless! Kill! Kill! Kill!'

Peter took up the huzzah, and Irene's hooves gouged the mud like shellfire. Grey streak, she overtook Lancer George, overtook them all, with Peter snatched along. She tore through smoke and iron rain and thickets of wire, over trenches crowded with woodlouse-grey Austrians. Peter lashed out with the sabre, catching a foreigner by the face, and Irene reared in celebration. A grenade exploded and took her eyes and she fell, horsemeat. Peter was thrown into a shell hole. He lay stunned, high riding boots in a sludge of dead men.

Red day turned to cold black night. Horses and men wept in the distance. Two pairs of jackboots crunched towards him, and he was dragged from his hole by his webbing. The jutting brows of two Austrian helmets crowded his faint and delirious gaze. Foreign words. An order. One left him, the other raised his rifle.

Peter spoke no German. 'Moment, s'il vous plaît'. His executioner paused and Peter reached into his tunic. A cigarette case, but no cigarette. Mary\'s portrait. A flare burst overhead, and her black-blonde hair shone. Like a magic painting, with every horror that Peter saw, Mary looked more beautiful. He suddenly felt no fear of death, but an invincible certainty that when the Austrian fired he would wake up in his valley. The jackboot stamped, an inch from his nose. The barrel twitched and fired. The bullet, like a million others, sank into the mud. Peter did not wake up in his valley. The Austrian crouched.

'We are called to attack.' He spoke English. 'A penal battalion for Slovene nationalist agitators.' His voice was bitter, but familiar. 'We will all die.' He put his rifle down, gently, and with slim fingers took Peter's watch, looked at it for a moment. 'When that is 10 o'clock, we are all dead. It will be safe to cross, back to your lines.' He pushed back his helmet, just a little. It showed cauliflower ears, a broken nose. He had tears in his eyes. His commander barked from the darkness. Dalibor, unsteady on his feet, left his friend in the mire of the shell hole and in the glittering light of the flare.

Alchemist

ALCHEMIST

Silverside Ray was described by those who did not know him very well as a \'proper London geezer\'. No one knew Silverside Ray very well. Silverside Ray owned a gym and a butcher's shop, adjoining each other and next to a train station which brought in City boys from Dalston and Hackney and further.

Had he any hair, it would have been silver. Silverside Ray was seventy. But he was broad-shouldered and thick-forearmed from carrying carcasses and shanks and sides of rib, and from lifting iron barbells in the gym. He had done both of these things for sixty years, hated them both, and wished for more.

The people who called Silverside Ray a 'proper London geezer' were excited by him because they felt their lives lacked authenticity, for they themselves were neither proper nor London nor geezers. They were City boys of the new school, twenty-five and thirty-five and forty-five year old boys from the Home Counties or Scotland who went to non-London universities and got degrees and then went to the City because that was how you made the money. They were men in tight, expensive, bad suits which wore thread-bare at the thighs, and for whom novelty and parody was the closest they got to authenticity: moustaches like First World War officers, which they wore as jokes. Shirts by Turnbull and Asser because James Bond wore them, and Omegas and Rolexes for the same reason, and which they ignored because they spent all day looking at screens which displayed the time precisely, synced to the atomic clock in Nevada Labs, because a fraction of a second's delay in trade volumes could cost a fortune. Their worlds felt unreal, so they wished Silverside Ray into reality.

They needed protein, to bulk, to become real and manifest like Ray, and they were constantly calculating grammes of protein per kilogramme of body. They could afford any fad powder or herb or potion. But what they really loved was what Silverside Ray sold them, which, unknown to them, was any part of the cow which Londoners did not eat. It was a good deal for Ray: he got ungutted carcasses cheap, only paying the abattoir for the flaying. He told the abattoir he pickled guts in formaldehyde for schools.

As he sold flank steak and rib out the front of his shop, he had cauldrons boiling hoof. He told the boys at the gym that Cossacks drank the jelly when they fought Napoleon, and he sold it in corked glass milk bottles, for authenticity. He cut oesophagus and trachea finely and dehydrated them in his ovens and sold them as 'protein chips'. Contemptuous of the boys' pointless maleness he peeled uterus, cervix, ovary apart. Salted and fried, he said his dad had called it 'German jerky' because he had eaten it at Sword Beach in '44. Bullock penises were easy to sell; when dogs ate them they were called pizzles. But dried and seasoned and chewy and sold as 'shin slices', the boys said it was better than chorizo.

Silverside Ray was no dinosaur: he had a smartphone, and followed TikTok fitness trends. He talked about macros, lying through his teeth about magnesium and potassium. He understood bulking and shredding. He faked sympathy with the scrawny, callow youths who knew they spent too long at their computers, and when he gave them glass jars with gingham lids rattling with dried cow brain, resembling coral growths, they looked at him like an uncle. He said it was a type of tripe, and the boys, missing home, thought of the home-cooked meals they had turned their noses up at, and they ate brains.

Silverside Ray was an alchemist. He took offal and transformed it to gold. The boys took gold and turned it to shit. The more they bought, the more he detested them. The richer he got, the more reckless.

Joey had never wanted to work in the City. He had wanted to be a doctor. He missed his grades, but was a bright boy. He got an internship with one of the banks instead. His boss, Marwood, told him about a gym. It was authentic, he was told. There were geezers there, he was told. There were lorry tires to flip, and bare concrete. When the trains passed, the weights on the rack rattled. It was proper. Joey wanted this authenticity: he had wanted to be in Medecins San Frontieres, he told himself, now this was no longer a possibility.

Better people than Joey were fooled by Silverside Ray. On the first of the month Ray sold prairie oysters: why would a man cheerfully and overtly selling bull testicles lie about the German jerky? So when Joey warned Marwood that he was eating bovine uterus, that he could tell by the distinctive cilia, Marwood got angry, and refused to believe him. Marwood told Ray and they laughed at the boy Joey. When Marwood let slip Joey had wanted to be a doctor, Silverside Ray got sly. He asked Marwood to bring the boy again. They'd have a man-to-man in his office. Marwood thought Silverside Ray might be a good influence, for he was a proper London geezer.

Joey was sly too. He came at Marwood's suggestion, but without him. He apologised. He joked he'd failed biology. Ray said he had no qualifications whatsoever, and they laughed. Joey paid for a jar of brains with cash. He said he was bulking. He planned to take the jar to the Food Standards Agency. Ray unscrewed the lid, and took a brittle brown puck from the jar. He offered it Eucharist-like and Joey ate it. Ray was impressed, and considered, briefly, letting the boy live.

Night. The screams were clearest at night. Gobbled up at Troy, borne on the southern wind, they rained over Philoctetes, king, cripple, maroon.

Nine years of exile on Lemnos left him a withered reflection of the king he had been. His kingdom collapsed to the foot of a stubborn apple tree slouching over a gritty beach. His bony archer's shoulders ached, lopsided from the draw, but worse were his legs, blackened and skeletal and suppurating. Hera had ripped them, never to heal, only to stink, forcing his comrades to cast him away

He no longer had the strength to drag himself to the nearby stream, instead he licked the dew from his shield like a thirsty dog. Then he took up a handful of sand, and began to scrub at the bronze. He burnished it to a mirror, but, gorgon-like, avoided his reflection. He had been renowned for his beauty, a credible suitor for sweet Helen. He did not want to know what had become of his face. He knew what had happened to his soul.

The sun was up, and with it the army of flies formed ranks and marched up his back, his face, his weeping legs. They stung, a thousand tiny swordsmen attacking their own Troy. He failed to catch one, and raised his shield to his lap.

Every day, all day, he used his warrior's shield to betray his countrymen. He knew, god-ordained and oath-bound, the Greeks would not win their war if he were rescued. But the heat, the thirst, the flies, the stench, the deformation and the degradation and the loneliness which pummelled him like heavy waves, all were too much. Night Of The Wasps

One year, when I was a child, wasps built a nest in the kitchen-wall vent. At first, I liked it. It blocked dad's smoke; he would stand outside the kitchen and enjoy a cigarette and, lordly, admire his little house and his little family.

The nest was a bulbous, papery thing. It hummed in the summer, quietened in a cold autumn and revived in winter as, Christmas luxury, we were allowed the heating for two hours daily. Black-and-yellow devils came creeping in. One stung the dog and the dog screamed and ran into a wall. It split its scalp. The vet's bill hurt. The wasps had to die.

But it was Christmas: exterminators were on holiday or charging holiday rates. We were poor. We were economising. Dad said he'd stopped smoking. So we wore long sleeves and hats and kept the kitchen cold. Our stitched-up dog hunted all day. It learnt to smash with paws, not to bite, after its black gums swelled with wasp poison. Dad, angry and cold-turkey, prowled with a badminton racquet. Mum cooked in her wedding veil and long bridal gloves, ghost of Christmas Past.

Christmas Eve: we put on our best clothes and walked to Midnight Mass. Mum used powder to cover her wasp stings. Dad lagged behind, breath fogging like cigarette smoke.

It was just the local church, a squat building made of stone always wet to the touch, but the glow of candlelight and the late hour gave it all a sense of barbarous pre-Roman power. It was cool inside, and everyone kept their coats on, adding to the pagan air. We shared a pew with another family, and the wood was icy through my best trousers. I prayed my own prayers, not following the priest's script. I begged God's help against the wasps. That they were also his creatures never occurred to me.

When the service ended, there was coffee in the too-hot hall and I complained I was tired and left mum and dad to talk to their church friends who had Old Testament names and children who did not go to my school.

I walked home by moonlight. In my memory, every Christmas Eve was lit bright as the inside of a silver bowl. In the cold air I could see a hundred miles. I had an ink-black clear-edged moon shadow. I stood alone in front of my house. Midnight, and all neighbours asleep or at church.

My lips began to ache, and the fumes made my heard spin. The fuel climbed the hose slow like treacle. Then it was in my mouth, an awful, adult smell suddenly turned to flavour. Retching, I filled the tray.

Moving the fuel was difficult. It sloshed left and right with each step like a live thing trying to escape. The kitchen vent was at the house's rear, the wasp nest bulging like a tumour.

I knew the fumes which made me woozy were lethal to the wasps. Their little lungs would fill and they would die. I raised the fuel-filled tray; I was short, the nest high, the effort made me grunt and petrol ran down my wrists and under my shirt and tickled my hairless armpits. They would defend their joy quite fiercely, pushing me away when I jumped onto the bed to be involved.

And I was so intensely focused on her, so desperate to hear that beloved heart resume, it was like I had gone deaf and all noise was gone from the world. He shook her, and she gurgled a little, wheezed a little, but these were the false noises of the false animals with squeakers, not the sounds of life. He sat on her bed and put his face in his hands and I think even he knew she had stopped.

I have stopped running, because my heart is breaking, and I can smell her very clearly for a moment. It is my collar. Some mote of her in my collar has risen to my nose, and she's there, and I am ashamed I lay in the river, adulterating her memory with duck-scent and grit. 

He finds me despairing, contorting, because I can't force my nose any closer to my collar. I am pleased to be with him, and it is almost like he is looking after me when he takes up the leash and strokes my ears. For a few paces, he must go first.

We walk for hours. It's hot, and the salt from his eyes mixes with the salt from his scalp. My paws ache and crack and grass seeds chafe between my toes. We have no water and my mouth tastes foul. Neither of us want to go home. We live in a haunted house now. I smell through time and space and when a door opens or a blanket is shaken out she is there again, and gone again. I feel her presence as a phantom pain. I can't bear her scent, nor bear to lose it.

I'm not naive about stopping. I came into the world smelling of it (I shared the womb with a stillborn brother). But most stopping has a little life to it: the hedgehog's quills twitch with maggots, the kingfisher's feathers shift under the tread of ants. Not so for her. She lay so completely and sterilely still, then, sealed in a scentless bag, was taken by men who smelt of nothing and whose saltless faces made them nearly invisible in a salt sea of spectators. How can so absolute a disappearance leave behind so many splinters?

He opens the door to our home. Her ghost is there, in the dark and the cool. He hesitates. He still has his leash in his hand. I will guide him in because he depends on me. I will go first.
