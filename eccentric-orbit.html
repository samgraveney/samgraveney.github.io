Eccentric Orbit

ECCENTRIC ORBIT

Cooper was born in 1933. In 1942 he started at a new school, in New Jersey: his previous one had begun to desegregate, and his father would not tolerate that. He was frightened on his first day. He remembered clearly seeing one boy, two years older, with red hair. A handsome boy. A boy who, though only 11, seemed like an adult.

That boy had a sister in Cooper's year. She called the boy 'Rip'. Cooper hated her lisp. The handsome boy was called Rich Adler.

There was a baking soda volcano in science class. It spewed on Cooper's trousers. He was sent out into the empty corridor to clean up. He turned a corner: there, Rich Adler, with his friends. Cooper did not hear what Rich said, but everyone laughed. He felt betrayed. He hated Rich Adler.

In 1950, Cooper's wish was to fly jet fighters. He didn't get into the Air Force. His father blamed it on desegregation. Cooper accepted this truth, because it meant it wasn't his fault.

Cooper worked in a radio factory. Shaping the tin cases made his hands bleed. There were always radios fizzing and snarling and snapping. He lived with his father.

One day, a radio came to life: 'Captain Richard 'Rip' Adler was awarded a Distinguished Flying Cross for shooting down two enemy planes over Pusan'. A lot of Cooper's colleagues were Pacific vets. They approved. Cooper slunk away, and puked.

Cooper became interested in the peace movement. No war in Korea, no 'Rip' Adler. When his father found flyers for peace, he broke Cooper\'s nose.

'It's a holy war, boy. I hope they drop the Bomb. It's us against the Reds,' his father warned, 'and the Jews.'

Cooper loved his father.

In 1955 Cooper got married. In 1958, he got divorced. He could not remember much about their marriage. A baby appeared, towards the end, and crawled out of his life. None of it had seemed real. Cooper stole a radio from work and began to learn the truth from strange broadcasts out of Oregon and zines out of California. He began to pull the threads together.

He learned about MKUltra, and mind control. He learned about the CIA and brainwashing.

He learned the Titanic and the Maine and the Lusitania were sunk by Jewish financiers for the insurance.

He followed with greasy excitement Joe McCarthy's crusading.

In 1963, when everyone else in the bar wept, he and his father shared warning looks when word came in from Dallas, Texas, that the Pope's president had been killed. They knew the truth, and that truth was dangerous.

It was 1967 when Cooper's father went to jail. He had been writing letters to politicians. He had threatened to murder Lyndon Johson. He took the rap for the letters Cooper had been sending to 'Rip' Adler. Cooper wanted Adler to know he would die in a plane crash. He wanted Adler to picture his legs disappearing in a gout of flame as a Vietcong rocket lit his jet fuel. Cooper did not know that Rip Adler was no longer in the Air Force. Rip Adler was an astronaut, and it was 1969 when the news announced he had walked on the moon.

Cooper knew this was a lie. He knew the footage had been faked on a sound studio by the Jew Kubrick. He could tell from the flag, waving in airlessness. He could tell by the shadows which did not make sense to him or any of his friends. Not that he had many of them, not since his father was locked up. Cooper was a very lonely man. He still, occasionally, wrote to Adler.

It was 1980 when Rip Adler got divorced from his high-school sweetheart. She had fallen in love with him 38 years earlier, when he had muttered 'poor kid', and had cracked a joke, distracting from a new boy who had wet himself. But going to the moon changed a man: no blacks were as black as the black of space, no whites as white as the white of the sun, no blues could be found on Earth as blue as the blue that was the world. Adler had seen so clearly from the moon's face that, since his return, by comparison he was blind and limited, and it had made him angry.

This anger was exacerbated by the letters he received every damn goddamn day telling him he'd never been on the moon, prophesying he would be killed in a plane crash, warning that he would die and die soon and die bad. Relentless. One had made him snap. He had struck his wife. She left.

It was 1985, and Cooper was already old. All intelligence and vigour burned up fighting shadows. His father had found Jesus in prison and had tried to turn Cooper from the conspiracies. But the pain of admitting a wasted life was too great to countenance. Cooper warned his father never to contact him again. That pain was manageable.

It was 1990. A documentary crew had come to Adler with a stinker of an idea, and a bag of cash. They'd found a moon-landing truther who had gone to Adler's school. This Cooper crank, and Adler, still handsome, still respected, but known to be difficult, would debate. Adler would rip Cooper to shreds. The producers had charts and diagrams and moon rocks. It would be good TV.

By the end of the shoot Cooper was nearly in tears. The veil was lifted. Man had walked on the moon. Fifty-seven years old, and he was seeing the world as clearly as Adler had, high above, looking down. He had so much lost time to make up for, so much hostility and meanness. He wished to thank Adler.

Rip Adler squinted at the man. Wondered why the name 'Cooper' was familiar to him. Then he remembered the name on the letter which had ended his marriage. Cooper was blubbering something about seeing clearly. Rip wanted to blind him.

He extended a still-strong hand, and took Cooper's brittle fingers in a crush. It was all filmed. It would make a good epilogue. Adler pulled Cooper into an embrace. And he whispered in his ear:

'It's all lies, Cooper. All faked. We've never walked on the moon.' Cooper recoiled. Adler stared into his milky, baffled eyes. He pulled him in again, for a final whispered warning: 'and no-one will ever believe I told you this.'

And he released Cooper to spin out, into the void.
